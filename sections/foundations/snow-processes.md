# Snow processes

Every parcel of glacier ice begins as snow, and between the falling crystal and its burial as firn the snow passes through a set of surface processes that fix the density, the grain size, and the layering the deeper ice inherits. This chapter follows snow through its first season, from the form in which it falls, through the metamorphism that reshapes it on the ground, the wind that redistributes and packs it, and the melt that coarsens it, to the point at which snow that has survived a summer becomes firn and the slow densification of {doc}`snow-to-ice` takes over. The melt that drives several of these processes is produced by the surface energy balance of {doc}`../thermomechanics/surface-energy-balance`. The subject is treated in Chapter 2 of {cite}`cuffey2010`, and the metamorphism specifically by {cite}`colbeck1982`.

## Snowfall and crystal form

Snow crystals grow in clouds by the deposition of water vapour onto a nucleus, and the form they take is set by the temperature and the humidity at which they grow. The same hexagonal symmetry of the ice lattice of {doc}`ice-structure` underlies every form, but small changes in conditions switch the growth between flat plates, six-armed stars, hollow columns, and slender needles, the systematic dependence first mapped by Nakaya and visible in the snow crystal of {numref}`fig-snow-crystal`. Crystals that fall through a region of supercooled droplets collect a coating of frozen droplets, called rime, and heavily rimed crystals become the rounded pellets known as graupel.

The form controls the density of the fresh deposit. Fresh snow has a density between roughly 50 and 400 kg per cubic metre, lowest for the cold, calm, dendritic snow that falls as open, intricate stars and highest for wind-broken or rimed grains that pack tightly on the ground. This is already a wide range, and the metamorphism, wind, and melt that follow narrow and raise it.

## Dry-snow metamorphism

A fresh snow crystal is far from equilibrium, since its sharp points and thin arms carry a large surface area and a high surface curvature. Vapour pressure is higher over a convex, high-curvature surface than over a flat or concave one, so water vapour moves from the points of the crystals to the necks and hollows between grains, and the crystals round off while the contacts between them grow into bonded necks. This rounding and bonding is sintering, the same premelted-surface transport met in {doc}`ice-structure`, and it both strengthens the snow and lets it settle, carrying the density toward about 550 kg per cubic metre by grain rearrangement alone.

The rounding just described is the equilibrium form of metamorphism, the form a snowpack at uniform temperature takes. A seasonal snowpack is rarely at uniform temperature, since its base sits near 0 °C over the ground or the older snow while its surface follows the cold winter air, and the resulting temperature gradient drives a second, opposite kind of metamorphism. Vapour moves up the temperature gradient from the warmer grains below to the colder grains above and deposits there, building faceted rather than rounded crystals, and where the gradient is strong and sustained it grows the large, hollow, cup-shaped crystals called depth hoar. Depth hoar is weakly bonded and mechanically fragile, the classic weak layer on which slab avalanches release, and because it forms most readily in thin, cold snowpacks it is a signature of the low-accumulation interiors of the ice sheets.

## Wind

Wind reworks snow more thoroughly than any other surface process. Above a threshold speed it lifts grains into saltation and suspension, fragmenting the delicate crystals into small, dense particles and redistributing them from exposed ridges into hollows and lee slopes. Where wind-driven grains are packed and bonded they form hard, dense wind slab, and where the surface is eroded it is sculpted into the ridges and grooves called sastrugi. On the polar ice sheets a large fraction of the snow that falls is moved at least once by wind before it is buried, which is one reason point measurements of accumulation are difficult to reconcile with the snowfall a climate model delivers, and the wind-packed layers laid down through a year are part of the stratigraphy that later densifies to depth.

## Melt metamorphism and the snowpack

Where the surface energy balance of {doc}`../thermomechanics/surface-energy-balance` brings the snow to the melting point, liquid water appears between the grains and metamorphism accelerates sharply. Water rounds and clusters the grains within hours to days, coarsening the snow far faster than dry metamorphism, and each cycle of melting and refreezing welds the grains into denser, harder layers and builds the icy crusts that mark old melt surfaces. Meltwater that percolates into cold snow below refreezes there, releasing latent heat and building the ice lenses and layers that define the percolation and wet-snow facies of {doc}`snow-to-ice`, and the runoff that does not refreeze enters the surface hydrology of {doc}`../thermomechanics/surface-hydrology`.

A seasonal snowpack is therefore a layered record of the weather that built it, with summer surfaces of coarse melt grains, winter layers of fine wind-packed snow, and crusts and hoar marking particular events. That stratigraphy, preserved as the snow is buried and compacted, is the annual layering that ice cores count and that the densification of the next chapter carries to depth.

## From snow to firn

Snow that has survived at least one melt season is, by definition, firn. By that stage the metamorphism, settling, and wind packing of this chapter have carried its density into the range of roughly 400 to 550 kg per cubic metre, near the limit of what grain rearrangement alone can achieve. The further transformation, the slow burial densification by which firn compacts to glacier ice and seals the atmosphere into bubbles, is the subject of {doc}`snow-to-ice`.
