# Observing present-day ice

The modeling chapters treated observations as given. A velocity mosaic was there to be inverted, a bed map to be run on, a thinning rate to be matched. Each of those numbers was a measurement that somebody made, and these chapters are about how the measurements are made and how far they can be trusted. They also answer a question the models cannot pose from inside themselves, whether the ice sheets are gaining or losing mass at this moment and how fast, the present-day accounting that is the empirical companion to the diagnostic problem of the earlier chapters. The observing of ice has grown in a single generation from a scatter of stakes in the snow into a dense web of satellites and ground instruments, and what follows surveys the main techniques, the physics each one rests on, the way its raw data become a glaciological quantity, and what it has revealed about the state of the ice today.

The methods are best organized by the quantity they recover. The first is the geometry of the ice surface and its change in time, the thickness and slope that drive the flow and the elevation change that signals imbalance, measured from orbit by the laser altimetry of {doc}`laser-altimetry-lab`, the radar altimetry of {doc}`radar-altimetry-lab`, and the stereo photogrammetry of {doc}`panchromatic-imagery-lab`. The second is the velocity of the surface, the field a diagnostic model must reproduce and from which the bed is inferred, mapped by the radar interferometry of {doc}`insar-lab` and by tracking features between optical images. The third is the internal structure of the ice and the state of its bed, sounded by the ice-penetrating radar of {doc}`../radar/em-waves`, {doc}`../radar/apres`, and {doc}`../radar/radiowave-fabric`. The fourth is what lies at and beneath the bed, the water, sediment, and rock that set the basal boundary condition, probed by the passive seismology of {doc}`cryoseismicity-lab`, the magnetotellurics of {doc}`magnetotellurics-lab`, and the gravimetry of {doc}`gravity-lab`. The geometry and velocity are mature products, consumed routinely by every model in the earlier chapters. The bed and sub-bed measurements are the observations we would use far more than we do, because they sense exactly the processes the models still parameterize rather than predict, the friction, the subglacial water, the crystal fabric, and any reader looking for an open problem should linger on those chapters.

Two broad families run through the part. Remote sensing from satellites and aircraft gives wide, repeated coverage of the surface and the shallow subsurface, and it is what turned glaciology into a science of whole ice sheets watched through time. Ground-based geophysics gives depth, since seismic, electromagnetic, and gravity methods reach through the ice to the bed and below, at the cost of covering only the few places an expedition can reach. The two are complementary, the satellites mapping the surface expression of a process and the ground geophysics the machinery beneath, and the strongest results usually come from combining them.

The spirit of these chapters is deliberately different from the modeling chapters. There the data arrived as finished products, a velocity mosaic, a bed map, a thinning rate, used without asking where they came from. Here each chapter develops the physics of one method and then works real data through to the glaciological product in a lab in the same document, starting from the raw measurement, the photon returns, the radar waveforms, the complex images, the seismograms, the magnetic time series, and processing it into exactly the products the models consume, so that each map can be read as what it is, a measurement with assumptions and failure modes rather than ground truth. The labs work on raw or near-raw data throughout, in the pattern the ApRES lab of {doc}`../radar/apres` sets, where a folder of radar chirps becomes a basal-melt time series one processing step at a time.
