# Basal motion: sliding and subglacial water

The velocity measured at the surface of a glacier is the sum of two contributions. One is the internal deformation of the ice column described by Glen's flow law. The other is basal motion, the bodily movement of the ice over or with the material at its bed. In the slow interior of an ice sheet, deformation dominates. In the ice streams and outlet glaciers that discharge most of the ice reaching the ocean, basal motion dominates, and it is the largest single source of uncertainty in projections of ice-sheet change. Basal motion is the subject of Chapter 7 of {cite}`cuffey2010`.

## Sliding over a rigid bed

Basal motion requires a bed at the pressure-melting point, because liquid water at the interface is what allows the ice to move past the bed rather than freeze to it. {cite}`weertman1957` described how ice slides over a hard, bumpy bed by two mechanisms acting together. On the upstream side of a small bump the ice is under higher pressure, which lowers the melting point and produces meltwater; that water flows around the bump and refreezes on the downstream side, releasing its latent heat. This process, called regelation, moves ice efficiently past small obstacles. Larger bumps are bypassed instead by enhanced creep, since the increased stress around them makes the ice deform faster. Small obstacles are handled best by regelation and large ones by creep, so there is an intermediate bump size that controls the resistance the bed can offer.

The result is a sliding relation in which the sliding speed grows with the basal shear stress, often written as a power law,

$$
u_b = C\,\tau_b^{\,m},
$$

where $\tau_b$ is the stress the bed exerts on the ice, and $C$ and $m$ depend on the roughness of the bed. This form is the simplest of the sliding laws used in ice-flow models.

## Water pressure and effective pressure

The simple power law leaves out the strong effect of subglacial water. The variable that matters is the effective pressure, the difference between the pressure of the overlying ice and the pressure of the water at the bed,

$$
N = p_i - p_w.
$$

When the water pressure is high and the effective pressure is low, the ice is partly floated off its bed, the contact stresses are reduced, and the glacier slides faster. Many sliding laws therefore include the effective pressure, for example by dividing the power law by a power of $N$. A further refinement recognizes that the bed cannot supply unlimited resistance: above some sliding speed the stress saturates at a Coulomb limit proportional to $N$, which is important for modeling fast flow near grounding lines.

## Deformable beds and drainage

Not every fast glacier sits on hard rock. Several of the large Antarctic ice streams rest on a layer of weak, water-saturated sediment called till, which deforms almost like a plastic material, yielding at a nearly constant stress that depends on the effective pressure rather than on the sliding speed. Where the bed is till, the distinction between sliding and bed deformation blurs, and the behavior is closer to Coulomb friction than to a viscous power law.

In both cases the controlling quantity is the subglacial water system. Water generated by basal melt and delivered from the surface collects at the bed, and the way it is stored and routed sets the water pressure and therefore the resistance to flow. The drainage can take the form of a distributed network of linked cavities at low discharge or of efficient channels melted into the ice at high discharge, and it evolves through the melt season. This coupling between hydrology and sliding is one of the active frontiers of glacier modeling, and it returns in the bridging chapters. The instability of marine ice sheets, in which a retreating grounding line on a reverse-sloped bed can lead to runaway loss, depends directly on how basal resistance is represented {cite}`schoof2007`.
