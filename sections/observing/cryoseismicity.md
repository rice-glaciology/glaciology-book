# Passive seismology and cryoseismicity

For all its slow creep, ice is a noisy material. As a glacier slides, fractures, and calves, it radiates seismic waves, and a seismometer set on or near the ice records a near-continuous stream of small earthquakes, the icequakes that are the subject of cryoseismology {cite}`podolskiy2016,aster2017`. Unlike the remote-sensing methods of the preceding chapters, which view the surface from above, passive seismology listens to the ice from the ground and hears processes that leave no surface trace, above all what happens at the bed. Because the waves are generated by the very processes a flow model must represent, sliding, fracture, and water flow, seismology is a direct window onto the mechanics of {doc}`../thermomechanics/basal-motion` and {doc}`../thermomechanics/hydrology`. This chapter surveys what the ice radiates and how it is read.

## The sources of icequakes

The seismic events fall into a few families by mechanism. Basal events come from the bed slipping in sudden increments rather than gliding smoothly, a stick-slip motion in which stress builds while the bed is locked and releases in an abrupt slip that radiates a wave; these reveal directly whether a glacier slides steadily or in jerks, and how that is modulated by the tide and the season. Surface and englacial events come from fracture, the opening of crevasses and the hydrofracture of water-filled cracks driving toward the bed, each cracking event sending out a pulse. Calving generates both ordinary local events and, for the largest tidewater glaciers, the glacial earthquakes large enough to be recorded by the global seismic network as a whole iceberg capsizes against its parent. Water itself is a source, the turbulent flow of subglacial and supraglacial streams producing a sustained tremor whose strength tracks the discharge. Sorting the catalogue of events by these mechanisms is the first task of analysis.

## Reading the records

The processing borrows the tools of earthquake seismology. Events are detected by watching for a short-term jump in the seismogram's energy against its longer-term background, located by the differing arrival times of the waves at a network of sensors, and characterized by the polarity and amplitude pattern of their first motions, which gives the orientation and type of the source. The spectrum of an event separates fracture from stick-slip from tremor. A complementary approach uses not the discrete events but the ambient vibration of the ice: cross-correlating the continuous noise between two sensors recovers the surface waves travelling between them, and their speed gives the structure of the firn and ice in between, while active seismic shots reflected from the bed reveal whether it is hard rock or soft, water-saturated till. A data-processing lab for this chapter detects and locates icequakes from a record using the short-term against long-term energy test.

## What it reveals

The method has overturned the picture of how some ice streams move. Whillans Ice Stream in West Antarctica, long thought to glide smoothly, was found to move almost entirely in twice-daily slip events triggered by the ocean tide, the whole stream lurching forward in seconds and then locking, a stick-slip behaviour invisible to any surface measurement of average velocity. Glacial earthquakes from Greenland's calving fronts have provided a running tally of the largest calving events and their increase as the ice sheet has warmed. Subglacial tremor maps where and when water moves at the bed, tying the seismology back to the drainage physics of {doc}`../thermomechanics/hydrology`, and basal reflections distinguish the slippery till beds from the frozen ones that the friction laws of {doc}`../thermomechanics/basal-motion` treat so differently. Seismology, alone among the observing methods, measures the bed processes as they happen.
