# Permafrost

Winter outlasts summer in the ground across a quarter of the Northern Hemisphere's land surface, and the result is land frozen year-round, not as glaciers but as frozen ground. Permafrost is defined thermally, as ground that remains at or below 0 °C for at least two consecutive years, and that definition makes this chapter a direct application of the heat conduction physics of {doc}`../thermomechanics/thermal-structure`, with the glacier removed and the soil left behind. The comprehensive treatment of the material in this chapter is {cite}`french2017`.

## The thermal structure of frozen ground

Ground temperature is a competition between the seasons above and the Earth's heat below, and both competitors are simple. The annual temperature cycle at the surface penetrates downward as a damped, lagged wave, the same conduction solution that erases the seasonal signal in the upper meters of a glacier, decaying with an e-folding depth of a few meters in soil. Below roughly fifteen to twenty meters, the depth of zero annual amplitude, the seasons are gone and the temperature follows the geothermal gradient, warming by about one degree for every thirty meters of depth.

Everything about the architecture of permafrost reads off the trumpet diagram. The **active layer** is the zone where the summer maximum exceeds 0 °C, the half meter to few meters that thaws each year, and it is the only layer where plants root and water moves freely. The top of permafrost is the base of the active layer. The **base of permafrost** lies where the geothermal gradient carries the mean profile back to the melting point, so colder surfaces make deeper permafrost. With a mean surface temperature of −10 °C the base sits near 300 m, and in the coldest ground in the Canadian high Arctic and Siberia permafrost reaches 400 to 600 m or more. Toward the warm margin of the permafrost zone the same arithmetic thins it to a few patchy meters, giving the familiar map classes of continuous, discontinuous, and sporadic permafrost. Bodies of water punch holes in it, since a lake that does not freeze to its bottom keeps the ground beneath near 4 °C; the unfrozen chimneys and pockets that result are called taliks.

The upper hundred meters of a permafrost borehole is also a recording thermometer. A change in surface temperature diffuses downward slowly, so today's profile carries the smoothed history of past surface temperatures, and boreholes across northern Alaska show a systematic curvature in their upper 100 to 150 m recording roughly a century of warming, registered in the ground before instrumental records existed in the Arctic {cite}`lachenbruch1986`. The same borehole paleothermometry, applied through an ice sheet, returns in {doc}`../climate/paleoclimate`.

## Ground ice and the landforms it builds

What distinguishes permafrost terrain from merely cold ground is ice in the pore space and beyond it, in lenses, wedges, and massive bodies that can outweigh the soil hosting them. The landforms of the periglacial world are the visible work of this ice. Thermal-contraction cracking of winter ground, infilled each spring by meltwater that refreezes, ratchets ice wedges wider year by year and tiles the tundra with polygonal patterned ground. Pingos, the conspicuous ice-cored hills of the Mackenzie Delta and Siberia, grow where a drained or infilled lake lets permafrost reinvade its talik from all sides, pressurizing the trapped pore water until it intrudes upward and freezes, jacking the surface into a dome. Rock glaciers, the lobate ice-rock mixtures creeping down mountain slopes at centimeters to a couple of meters per year, are the periglacial expression of the same ice creep treated in {doc}`../ice_flow/ice-rheology`, with debris along for the ride. And where ice-rich permafrost thaws, the ground settles unevenly into the pits, hollows, and lake-pocked chaos called thermokarst.

A heated building thaws its own foundation and founders; the cracked and tilted structures of Siberian and Alaskan towns are thermokarst in miniature. The Trans-Alaska Pipeline carries oil that leaves the ground at 80 °C across 1,300 km of mostly frozen ground, and survives by refusing to share its heat, elevated on supports whose passive heat-pipe radiators pump warmth out of the ground in winter to keep the permafrost frozen.

## The permafrost carbon reservoir

The reason permafrost has moved from an engineering specialty to a centerpiece of climate science is what it stores. Northern permafrost soils hold on the order of 1,000 Gt of organic carbon, more than the roughly 800 Gt now in the atmosphere, accumulated because frozen ground is where dead plant matter goes to not decompose {cite}`schuur2015`. Thaw turns the freezer off. Microbial decomposition resumes, releasing carbon dioxide where the soil drains and methane where it stays waterlogged, and the warming this produces deepens thaw in turn, a slow positive feedback on global climate with the same logical shape as the ice-albedo feedback of {doc}`sea-ice`. Offshore, relict permafrost beneath the shallow East Siberian shelf, drowned by postglacial sea-level rise, caps methane hydrates whose stability depends on staying cold, an additional reservoir under a warming, ice-losing Arctic Ocean.

Mars is a permafrost planet, with a mean surface temperature near −50 °C, polygonal patterned ground photographed at high latitudes, and clean water ice excavated centimeters below the soil by the Phoenix lander, the same periglacial signatures this chapter has described, legible on another world.
