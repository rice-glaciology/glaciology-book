# Observing the cryosphere

By this point in the book you have solved the diagnostic problem, inverted it, and marched it forward in time, and every number those models consumed, the geometry they ran on, the velocities their inversions fit, the thinning their forcing was tuned against, was an observation somebody made. These chapters are about where those numbers come from and how far to trust them, and about the harder question a forecast cannot dodge, whether the ice sheets are in balance right now. The observing of ice has grown in a generation from a few stakes in the snow into a dense web of satellites and ground instruments. The chapters that follow survey the main techniques, what each one measures, how the raw data are turned into a glaciological quantity, and what they have revealed. Each method has its own chapter; this one lays out the landscape.

It helps to organize the methods by what they measure. Four quantities matter most. The first is the geometry of the ice surface and how it changes, which gives the thickness and slope that drive the flow and the elevation change that signals imbalance. It is measured from orbit by laser altimetry in {doc}`laser-altimetry`, by radar altimetry in {doc}`radar-altimetry`, and by stereo photogrammetry from high-resolution panchromatic imagery in {doc}`panchromatic-imagery`. The second is the motion of the surface, the velocity field that a diagnostic model must reproduce and from which the bed is inferred. It is mapped by radar interferometry in {doc}`insar` and by tracking features between optical images. The third is the internal structure of the ice and the properties of its bed, sounded by the ice-penetrating radar of {doc}`../radar/em-waves`, {doc}`../radar/apres`, and {doc}`radiowave-fabric`. The fourth is what lies at and beneath the bed, the water, sediment, and rock that set the basal boundary condition, probed by the passive seismology of {doc}`cryoseismicity`, the magnetotellurics of {doc}`magnetotellurics`, and the gravity measurements of {doc}`gravity`. The first two are mature products, consumed routinely by every model in the earlier chapters. The last two are the observations we would like to use far more than we do, because they sense exactly the processes, friction, subglacial water, fabric, the violence of calving, that the models of this book still parameterize rather than predict, and any reader looking for an open problem should linger on those chapters.

Two broad families run through these chapters. Remote sensing from satellites and aircraft gives wide, repeated coverage of the surface and the shallow subsurface, and it is what turned glaciology into a science of whole ice sheets observed through time. Ground-based geophysics gives depth: seismic, electromagnetic, and gravity methods reach through the ice to the bed and below, at the cost of covering only the few places an expedition can reach. The two are complementary, the satellites mapping the surface expression of a process and the ground geophysics revealing the machinery beneath, and the strongest results in the field usually come from combining them. The spirit of these chapters is therefore deliberately different from the modeling chapters. There the data arrived as finished products, a velocity mosaic, a bed map, a thinning rate, consumed without questioning where they came from. Here we start from the raw measurement, photon returns, radar waveforms, complex images, seismograms, magnetic time series, and process it ourselves into exactly those products, so that every map the models consumed can be read as what it is, a measurement with assumptions and failure modes rather than ground truth. The labs in these chapters work on raw or near-raw data throughout, in the pattern the ApRES lab of {doc}`../radar/apres-lab` sets, where a folder of radar chirps becomes a melt-rate time series one processing step at a time.
