# Glacial geomorphology

The Laurentide, Cordilleran, and Fennoscandian ice sheets melted thousands of years before anyone could measure them, yet we speak confidently about their extent, thickness, flow directions, and even the temperature of their beds. This chapter explains where that confidence comes from. Every erosional and depositional process of {doc}`erosion` leaves a form, and each form is an instrument reading from a vanished glacier. Glacial geomorphology is, in effect, the observational glaciology of {doc}`../observing/elevation` pointed backward in time.

The forward problem takes a glacier and predicts its products. Here we run it backward, from products to glacier, and the inversion is well posed because different ice properties write on the landscape in different, largely independent ways.

| Landform | What it records |
|---|---|
| Striations, drumlins, flutes, megascale lineations | Flow direction, and fast sliding where lineations are long |
| Roches moutonnées | Flow direction (abraded stoss, quarried lee) and a wet, sliding bed |
| Trimlines on valley walls and nunataks | Former ice-surface elevation, hence thickness |
| Cirque floors | Paleo-equilibrium-line altitude, hence paleoclimate |
| Terminal and lateral moraines | Ice extent at the dated moment of standstill |
| Erratics | Flow paths, from source outcrop to resting place |
| U-shaped valleys and fjords | Sustained wet-based flow concentrated along the trough |
| Molded rock beside weathered, undisturbed surfaces | The boundary between wet-based and cold-based ice |

## Direction, extent, thickness

Flow direction is the easiest reading. Striations record it at the scale of centimeters, drumlins at the scale of a city. The streamlined hills of Seattle all point along the path of the Puget Lobe, and identical megascale lineations on the floor of the Ross Sea, mapped by ships rather than lidar, mark the corridors of paleo-ice streams, the same association of streamlined beds with fast flow that {doc}`ice-sheets` described for modern ice. Far-traveled erratics extend the flow map across whole provinces. A granite boulder resting on plateau basalt, like the erratics strewn across the Waterville Plateau by the Okanogan Lobe, or the glacially delivered boulder sitting in a Seattle neighborhood, is a flow line with a known endpoint.

Extent comes from moraines, nested like high-water marks. The fresh, sharp ridges a few hundred meters beyond modern termini on Mount Baker date from the Little Ice Age; the great moraine loops impounding the lakes below the Tasman Glacier in New Zealand mark the last glacial maximum. Thickness comes from trimlines, the sharp elevation on a valley wall or nunatak separating glacially molded rock below from frost-shattered rock above, which fossilizes the former ice surface. Trimlines in the Ellsworth Mountains record an Antarctic ice sheet substantially thicker than today's {cite}`denton1992`, and cirque-floor elevations, clustering near the snowline that fed them, do the same job for paleo-ELAs that the mass-balance gradient does for modern glaciers in {doc}`../climate/glacier-variations`.

## Thermal regime and landform preservation

The subtlest reading is temperature. The erosion chapter ended with the observation that cold-based ice protects while wet-based ice consumes, and the converse turns preservation into a thermometer. Where bedrock is molded, striated, and plucked, the bed was at the melting point and sliding, as defined in {doc}`../thermomechanics/thermal-structure`. Where delicate weathered surfaces, old soils, and even century-old moraines sit undisturbed though ice demonstrably covered them, the bed was frozen. On Mount Hope in the Transantarctic Mountains, photographs taken during the heroic-age expeditions match modern ones boulder for boulder, and the juxtaposition of such surfaces with freshly molded rock nearby maps the patchwork of frozen and thawed beds beneath the former, thicker ice sheet. This is the basal thermal state of the heat-flow chapter, recovered from rocks alone.
