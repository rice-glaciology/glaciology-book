# The chemical composition of ice

The preface catalogued what is strange about water; this chapter begins the explanation at the most elementary level the book will use, the atoms themselves. The aim is to assemble, in one place, the background chemistry that the following chapters draw on freely, what an atom and an isotope are, why the water molecule has the formula it does, what kinds of bonds matter and how strong each one is, and what a real piece of glacier ice actually contains besides H₂O. A reader fresh from a chemistry course can skim to the energy ladder and move on; a reader for whom this is distant memory should find here everything the rest of the part takes for granted.

## Atoms and elements

An atom is a heavy, compact, positively charged nucleus surrounded by light, negatively charged electrons. The nucleus contains protons, each carrying one unit of positive charge, and neutrons, electrically neutral and nearly the same mass; the number of protons, the atomic number, fixes the charge of the nucleus, therefore the number of electrons in the neutral atom, and therefore all of the atom's chemistry. Ice is built from the first and the eighth entries in that sequence. Hydrogen, with one proton and one electron, is the simplest atom there is, and oxygen, with eight protons and eight electrons, is the third most abundant element in the universe and the most abundant in Earth's crust. Chemistry happens almost entirely among the outermost electrons, and the masses tell us where the substance resides, since the electrons contribute less than a twentieth of a percent of the mass of a water molecule. The mass is nuclear; the behavior is electronic.

Quantities of atoms are counted in moles, where one mole is $6.022\times10^{23}$ particles, the number chosen so that a mole of particles weighs, in grams, the particle's mass in atomic units. A mole of water is eighteen grams, a tablespoon or so, and the absurd size of the number is worth a pause, since it is why the statistical reasoning previewed in the preface works so well. Eighteen grams of ice is not a few molecules to be tracked but $6\times10^{23}$ of them to be averaged, and averages over numbers that large behave with the certainty of law.

## Why water is H₂O

Electrons around a nucleus fill orbitals in shells of definite capacity, for reasons {doc}`energy-levels` sets out, and an atom is chemically restless until its outermost shell is full. Hydrogen's single shell holds two electrons and contains one, so hydrogen is one electron short and forms exactly one covalent bond, in which two atoms complete their shells by sharing a pair of electrons between them. Oxygen's outer shell holds eight and contains six, so oxygen is two electrons short and forms exactly two covalent bonds. One atom that needs two bonds, joined to two atoms that each supply one, gives H₂O, and the bookkeeping that predicts the formula also predicts the cast of minor characters that recur throughout this book, the hydroxide fragment OH with one bond unsatisfied, and the ions H₃O⁺ and OH⁻ produced when a proton changes molecules. The shape of the molecule, the angle between the bonds, and the two unshared electron pairs left over on the oxygen are the subject of the next chapter; the formula needs nothing but counting to eight.

## Isotopes

Atoms of one element can differ in their number of neutrons, and these varieties are isotopes, identical in chemistry, different in mass. Hydrogen comes as ordinary ¹H and as deuterium, ²H or D, with one neutron, roughly one atom in 6,400 in natural water; oxygen comes overwhelmingly as ¹⁶O with a fifth of a percent of ¹⁸O and a trace of ¹⁷O. Natural ice is therefore a mixture of isotopic varieties of the water molecule, dominated by H₂¹⁶O but salted throughout with HDO and H₂¹⁸O. Because mass affects vibration and vibration affects volatility, the heavy varieties condense slightly more readily than the light ones, and that small physical difference, amplified by the atmosphere's repeated distillations, becomes the thermometer by which ice cores read past climate. The mechanism is developed quantitatively in the next chapter and harvested in {doc}`../climate/paleoclimate`; here the point is only that the composition of ice includes its isotopes, and that they carry information.

## Chemical bonds and the energy ladder

Atoms bind to one another in a small number of ways, and the rest of this book is, energetically speaking, a story about the rungs of one ladder. At the top sits the covalent bond, the shared electron pair, worth about 460 kJ/mol for the O-H bond inside a water molecule. Far below it sits the hydrogen bond, the electrostatic grip between a proton of one molecule and the unshared electrons of another, worth roughly 20 kJ/mol, the bond that holds ice together and the central character of this part. Below that again sit the van der Waals attractions present between any molecules whatever, the dipole, induction, and dispersion forces of the next chapter, worth a kJ/mol or so. And beneath the whole ladder runs the thermal energy of the preface, $RT \approx 2.3$ kJ/mol at the melting point, the budget against which every rung is measured. For converting between the chemist's and the physicist's units, one electron volt per molecule is 96.5 kJ/mol, so the covalent bond is about 4.8 eV, the hydrogen bond about 0.2 eV, and the thermal budget about a fortieth of an eV.

Reading the ladder explains the phases of water at a glance. Thermal energy at terrestrial temperatures is hopeless against the covalent bond, which is why water molecules survive as molecules in all three phases, never dissociating in bulk. It is roughly a tenth of a hydrogen bond, which is why those bonds hold firmly enough to build a crystal yet break often enough, a few molecules in ten thousand at any instant, for ice to creep, conduct protons, and trade molecules with the vapor. And it comfortably exceeds the van der Waals rung, which is why substances held only by dispersion forces, like the H₂S of the preface's comparison, are gases where water is solid. Ionic bonds, the wholesale transfer of electrons that builds salts like NaCl, complete the taxonomy; they matter here chiefly because water's polar molecules dismantle them, which is where sea salt comes from and why the ocean resists freezing in {doc}`../cryosphere/sea-ice`.

## The composition of natural ice

Real glacier ice is not pure H₂O, and the departures from purity, though minute, run several of this book's later chapters. Polar ice is among the purest solid materials found in nature, with total impurity contents measured in parts per billion to parts per million by weight, yet those traces are everything to the chapters that use them. Sea salt and sulfate arrive from the ocean and from volcanic eruptions, mineral dust from distant deserts, and acids from the atmosphere's chemistry, and all of them are largely rejected from the crystal lattice itself, since the lattice of the next two chapters has no comfortable site for foreign ions. They reside instead at grain boundaries and in the premelted liquid described in {doc}`ice-structure`, where they dominate the electrical conduction that radar senses and supply the volcanic time-markers that {doc}`../climate/paleoclimate` dates cores by. A few species can enter the lattice substitutionally, and those few act as dopants for the electrical defects of {doc}`point-defects`.

The other major ingredient is air. Glacier ice forms from snow, and the compaction described in {doc}`snow-to-ice` seals roughly ten percent of the ice's volume into bubbles of trapped atmosphere, which at sufficient depth and pressure dissolve into the lattice as air-clathrate inclusions. The bubbles are the reason glacier ice is white where pure ice is blue-tinged and transparent, and their contents are the only direct archive of the ancient atmosphere on Earth. Ice, in composition, is therefore four things at once, a crystal of light water, a dilute solution of its own heavy isotopes, a grain-boundary reservoir of atmospheric chemistry, and a sealed museum of old air. The next chapter supplies the quantum mechanics that fixes the energies quoted above; the chapter after it takes up the molecule that hosts all of it.
