# Gravity

Every method in this part so far has inferred mass change from geometry or motion; gravity is the observable that weighs the ice directly, because the mass itself is the signal. Two quite different uses of gravity have become central to glaciology. From orbit, satellites that sense the tiny time variations in the Earth's gravity field weigh the ice sheets directly and watch their mass change month by month. From aircraft, measurements of the static gravity field reveal the rock and water beneath the ice where radar cannot see, mapping the shape of the bed and the cavities the ocean fills. This chapter covers both. The first speaks to the mass balance of {doc}`../ice_flow/mass-balance`, the second to the bathymetry that controls ocean-driven melt and the stability of marine ice.

## Weighing the ice sheets from orbit

The GRACE mission and its successor flew two satellites in the same orbit, one chasing the other, and measured with extraordinary precision the tiny changes in the distance between them as they passed over regions of greater or lesser mass. As the lead satellite crossed a heavy region it was tugged forward and sped up, then the trailing satellite did the same, and the changing separation mapped the gravity field, and its change through time mapped the redistribution of mass at and near the surface {cite}`tapley2004`. Over an ice sheet that redistribution is dominated by the gain and loss of ice, so differencing the gravity field between months gives the change in ice mass directly, in gigatonnes, without any need for a density assumption or a firn model. This is the great strength of the method and the reason it is independent of the altimetry of {doc}`laser-altimetry` and {doc}`radar-altimetry`: those measure a change in volume and must convert it to mass, while gravity measures the mass change itself.

The two GRACE satellites flew roughly 220 km apart, both moving at about 7.5 km/s, and the ranging system tracked the separation between them at the micron level, a fraction of a human hair, between two objects crossing continents. GRACE-FO, flying since 2018, replaced the microwave ranging link with a laser interferometer that holds the noise floor to tens of nanometres. That ranging precision is what converts into the ability to detect the monthly gain or loss of a region's snowfall and ice, weighed from orbit.

## Processing and its one great correction

Turning the inter-satellite ranging into a mass trend for an ice sheet requires solving for the gravity field, representing it as a set of mass concentrations over the region, and removing the contributions of the atmosphere and the ocean and of water stored on the surrounding land. One correction dominates the uncertainty. The solid Earth is still slowly rebounding from the unloading of the great ice sheets of the last glacial maximum, and this glacial isostatic adjustment moves rock mass beneath the ice at a rate comparable to the present ice loss, so it must be modelled and subtracted before the ice signal is clean. That adjustment, and the sea-level accounting it belongs to, is left for separate treatment. A data-processing lab for this chapter takes a regional mass time series and separates a long-term trend from the seasonal cycle.

## Mapping the bed from the air

The second use of gravity reads the static field rather than its change. The ice, the water beneath a floating shelf, and the rock below all have different densities, so the gravity measured over an ice shelf or a glacier carries the shape of the seafloor and the structure of the basin. Where the ice is afloat or the bed is below sea level, radio-echo sounding struggles, because water absorbs the radar, and airborne gravity fills the gap, inverting the measured anomaly for the depth of the cavity the ocean occupies. These maps of sub-shelf bathymetry are what tell us whether warm ocean water can reach a grounding line, and they have been decisive in understanding the vulnerability of the Amundsen Sea glaciers. Mass change from above and bathymetry from gravity together connect the ice to the ocean that is melting it, closing the observing system that this part has surveyed and pointing toward the coupled prognostic problem the book turns to next {cite}`velicogna2006`.
