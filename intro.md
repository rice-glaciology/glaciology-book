# Introduction to Glaciology and Ice-Flow Modeling

Welcome to the Rice Glaciology group's open textbook. Here, we introduce the physics of glaciers and ice sheets and shows how to encdoe that physics into working numerical models using the open source finite element model package [icepack](https://icepack.github.io/).

The book is aimed at advanced undergraduates and beginning graduate students who have taken some calculus and physics, but who are new to glaciology. We build up the ideas in small steps try to introduce mathematics to physical problems and notebooks intended to gamify some of the learning. Throughout, the standard reference is *The Physics of Glaciers* {cite}`cuffey2010`; we point to specific chapters there for readers who want to go deeper.

## The first observers

The scientific study of ice is young, but the observation of ice is not, and the oldest recorded encounter with the Antarctic cryosphere belongs to Polynesian voyagers. In the oral histories of the Māori tribal groups Ngāti Rārua and Te Āti Awa, the navigator Hui Te Rangiora sailed south from the Cook Islands on the vessel Te Ivi o Atea around 650 CE, into waters his crew described as *Te tai-uka-a-pia*, the sea foaming like arrowroot, the scraped white powder of pia being the closest thing in a tropical Pacific world to the appearance of floe ice {cite}`wehi2021`. The accounts speak of a white land that was floating, of bare summits piercing the sky, of animals that dive to great depths. Read with a glaciologist's eye, these are observations of pack ice, ice shelves, and the marine life of the Southern Ocean, made and preserved roughly twelve centuries before the continent entered European charts. The connection runs unbroken to the present. Ngāi Tahu and other iwi hold these voyaging histories among their treasured knowledge; a carved pou by the Ngāi Tahu artist Fayne Robinson, Te Kaiwhakatere o te Taki, the Navigator of the Heavens, has stood at Scott Base since 2013 with its face turned to the sky that guided those voyages; and Māori scholars today argue for an Antarctic future managed with Indigenous frameworks alongside the science {cite}`wehi2021policy`.

Nor is the Southern Ocean the only such archive. Sámi reindeer herders in northern Fennoscandia maintain a taxonomy of snow far finer than anything in this book, dozens of named types distinguished by the density, hardness, and layering that decide whether animals can dig to forage, and those distinctions encode exactly the material properties our firn and snow chapters quantify. Communities in the Andes, the Himalaya, Alaska, and Greenland have watched, named, and depended on their glaciers for centuries. The instrumental record that this book leans on is the newest layer in a long human archive of ice, and it is worth knowing, before the equations begin, that the equations are not where the subject began.

## For whom, and by whom?

It is fair to ask of any science who it serves and who gets to do it, and recent work has asked exactly this of ours {cite}`robel2024`. The discipline that grew out of nineteenth-century Europe carried the shape of its origins. Field glaciology built a heroic self-image, expedition science led by men and mythologized as the conquest of hostile places, and that image hardened into a gatekeeping conflation of scientific ability with outdoorsmanship that has no basis in the physics; as late as 2009, fewer than a fifth of the authors in the field's flagship journals were women, and a feminist analysis of the field's history and framing landed only in the last decade {cite}`carey2016`. The discipline's map carries the same inheritance. The summit glacier of New Guinea sits on the homeland of the Amung people yet bears the name of Jan Carstenszoon, the Dutch East India Company explorer who sighted it in 1623 while kidnapping Indigenous people along his route, and Louis Agassiz, who gave glaciology its founding theory and his name to glaciers, lakes, and awards, also spent his scientific authority promoting racist pseudoscience, a legacy that has led to renamings such as the European Geosciences Union's decision to retitle its glaciology medal after Julia and Johannes Weertman.

The question of "for whom" has a sharp present tense. The people most exposed to the cryosphere's changes, downstream of shrinking Andean glaciers that supply dry-season water, in the path of outburst floods that have killed tens of thousands in Peru's Cordillera Blanca alone, on low coastlines already losing ground to the sea-level rise this book teaches you to project, are disproportionately Indigenous and rarely at the table where the science is framed, while demographic surveys of major polar collaborations remain overwhelmingly white {cite}`robel2024`. We keep this question open through the book rather than settling it in an introduction. The tools here are deliberately open, the code free, the models runnable on a laptop in a Docker container, because the cheapest thing a textbook can do about a gatekept field is to remove the gates it controls. For a deeper treatment, the [GeoContext](https://geo-context.github.io) teaching modules on glaciology's social history, which this section draws on, are a good place to start.

## How the book is organized

The order of the chapters follows a single pedagogical choice: the book is built around the distinction between the diagnostic and the prognostic problems of ice flow. The diagnostic problem is to find the velocity of a glacier whose shape is known at one instant. The prognostic problem is to evolve that shape forward in time. The first needs the physics of how ice resists stress; the second adds the physics of how ice gains and loses mass. We teach them in that order, and the labs run through the whole book in the same spirit, diagnostic model experiments in the early parts and prognostic experiments later.

A short **getting started** part introduces icepack, the Python package for glacier and ice-sheet flow modeling built on the finite element library [Firedrake](https://www.firedrakeproject.org/) {cite}`shapero2021`, and the Docker image that runs it, so that models are in your hands from the first week rather than saved for the end. The **physics of ice** then builds the material from the molecule up, the water molecule and its bonding, the open crystal that results, the lattice vibrations that carry heat and give temperature its statistical meaning, and the point defects hidden in the proton disorder. It is more chemistry than a flow modeler strictly needs, but it pays off, because the deformation of ice turns out to be a defect-mediated process and Glen's flow law is most honestly understood from the bottom up. **Ice deformation and flow** develops the language of stress and strain, the rheology that closes the system, the momentum balance and its hierarchy of approximations, and ends by solving the diagnostic problem with icepack on a synthetic ice shelf, twice, once forward and once as an inverse problem, because inferring what we cannot see from what we can measure is half of modern glaciology. **The bed and basal processes** bring in heat flow, which decides whether the bed is frozen or thawed, the sliding and subglacial water that a thawed bed permits, and a lab comparing friction laws on a Thwaites-like glacier, followed by the erosion that sliding ice works on the landscape.

The prognostic half begins with **mass balance and climate**, accumulation and ablation, firn, glacier response theory, the ice sheets and their instabilities, and the climate archive in the ice. Firn is deliberately held here rather than placed with the physics of ice at the front; it belongs to the surface and to climate, it records past atmospheres in ice cores, and it complicates the conversion of satellite elevation change into mass change. **Prognostic modeling and climate forcing** then makes the evolution problem concrete, the thickness equation as a nonlinear diffusion marched forward in time, the practical hundred-to-thousand-year horizon of such experiments, and the reduced models that carry the story beyond that horizon, opening from the recognition that on long timescales ice sheets are not driven by climate so much as they are a part of it. **Observing ice** follows, framed around the products that ice-flow models consume, velocities for inversions, elevations for mass balance, bed geometry from radar, and equally around the observations we would like to use more, the seismicity, electrical soundings, and phase-sensitive radar that sense processes at the bed we still do not understand well enough to model. The part works from the raw data up: where the modeling chapters used finished datasets, these chapters and their labs process actual measurements, photon clouds, waveforms, interferograms, seismograms, into those datasets, so the products stop being ground truth and become measurements with assumptions. **The wider cryosphere** closes the book with sea ice, the landscapes of former glaciers, permafrost, the ice of other worlds, where every tool built here is needed again, and finally sea level itself, the projection problem that carries everything in this book downstream to the people waiting on the answer.

Heat flow sits across the divide between the two problems. The temperature field evolves with the flow, which makes it prognostic, yet its quasi-steady consequence at the bed is exactly the boundary condition the diagnostic problem needs. We introduce it with the basal processes for that reason, and return to its full time evolution, coupled to climate, with the prognostic material.

## Running the code

The modeling chapters use icepack, which runs on Firedrake. Firedrake can be awkward to install directly, so this book ships a **Docker image** that gives you a ready-to-go environment with Firedrake, icepack, and JupyterLab. See {doc}`sections/modeling/running-icepack-docker`. Because that stack is heavy, the notebooks are **not executed** when the book is built — you run them yourself in the container.
