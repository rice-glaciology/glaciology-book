# The surface energy balance

The basal thermal state of {doc}`thermal-structure` was decided by a balance of fluxes at the bed, the geothermal flux and frictional heating set against conduction into the ice, with any surplus going into melt. The surface of a glacier is governed by the same kind of accounting, with a different set of fluxes. Sunlight, the exchange of longwave radiation with the atmosphere, the turbulent transfer of heat and moisture by the wind, and conduction into the ice below all meet at the surface, and when their sum is positive and the surface is already at the melting point, the excess melts ice. This chapter sets out that budget, the surface counterpart of the basal boundary condition, because it is what produces the meltwater whose refreezing shapes the firn of {doc}`../foundations/snow-to-ice` and whose routing to the bed is the subject of {doc}`surface-hydrology`. The surface energy balance is treated in Chapter 5 of {cite}`cuffey2010`.

## The surface as a boundary condition

The heat equation of {doc}`thermal-structure` governs the temperature throughout the thickness of a glacier, and it requires a condition at each end. At the bed that condition is the geothermal-plus-frictional balance written there. At the surface the condition is the energy balance with the atmosphere, which fixes either the surface temperature or, once the surface has reached the melting point, the rate of melt. The net energy available at the surface is the sum of the fluxes arriving there,

$$
Q_M = (1-\alpha) S_\downarrow + L_\downarrow - \varepsilon\sigma T_s^4 + H_S + H_L + Q_C,
$$

where $S_\downarrow$ is the incoming shortwave radiation and $\alpha$ the surface albedo, $L_\downarrow$ is the incoming longwave radiation from the atmosphere and $\varepsilon\sigma T_s^4$ the longwave emitted by a surface at temperature $T_s$, $H_S$ and $H_L$ are the turbulent fluxes of sensible and latent heat, and $Q_C$ is the conductive flux exchanged with the ice below. The signs are written so that a term is positive when it delivers energy to the surface. When the sum $Q_M$ is negative the surface cools and its temperature falls until the terms rebalance; when $Q_M$ is positive but the surface is below freezing the surface warms; and when $Q_M$ is positive and the surface has reached $0\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ the surface temperature can rise no further, and the surplus goes into melt at the rate

$$
\dot m = \frac{Q_M}{\rho_w L_f},
$$

with $L_f = 334\ \mathrm{kJ\,kg^{-1}}$ the latent heat of fusion. This is the exact surface analogue of the basal statement that once the bed reaches the melting point the surplus heat melts ice rather than warming it. The whole of surface melting is contained in deciding when $Q_M > 0$ at a surface held at the melting point, and the rest of this chapter is the anatomy of the terms that decide it.

## The radiative fluxes

The radiative terms are usually the largest in the budget, and the shortwave is the one over which the surface itself has the most control. The incoming shortwave $S_\downarrow$ is set by the Sun's elevation, the length of day, and the cloud cover, and over a melting glacier in summer it can exceed several hundred watts per square metre. What the surface absorbs, however, is only the fraction $1-\alpha$, and the albedo $\alpha$ ranges from about 0.9 for fresh dry snow to 0.3 or less for bare, dirty ice. The albedo is therefore the most important single property of a melting surface, since it can change the absorbed shortwave by a factor of several without any change in the Sun, and it is the reason a melting glacier behaves so differently once its winter snow cover has gone.

The longwave terms exchange energy with the atmosphere. The surface, being nearly a perfect blackbody in the infrared, emits $\varepsilon\sigma T_s^4$ with $\varepsilon$ close to one, which for a surface at the melting point is about $315\ \mathrm{W\,m^{-2}}$, a large and nearly constant loss. The atmosphere returns $L_\downarrow$, which depends strongly on cloud and on the temperature and humidity of the air, rising sharply under a warm, cloudy sky. The net longwave $L_\downarrow - \varepsilon\sigma T_s^4$ is usually a loss under clear skies and can approach balance under thick cloud, which is why an overcast night over a glacier is far less cold than a clear one.

## The turbulent fluxes

Wind carries heat and water vapour to and from the surface, and these turbulent fluxes are what couple the glacier to the weather. The sensible-heat flux $H_S$ transfers heat whenever the air is warmer than the surface, and the latent-heat flux $H_L$ transfers the latent heat of vaporization whenever water vapour condenses onto the surface or, with the opposite sign, is carried away by evaporation or sublimation. Both are commonly written in bulk-aerodynamic form, proportional to the wind speed and to the air-to-surface difference in temperature or in vapour pressure, with coefficients that depend on the roughness of the surface and on the stability of the air above it. Over a melting surface the air is typically warmer than the ice, so the sensible flux delivers heat, and when the air is also humid the latent flux adds condensation, the two together able to drive rapid melt even under cloud that suppresses the shortwave. This is the regime of the maritime glaciers of Norway, New Zealand, and Patagonia, where melt is delivered largely by warm wet air rather than by sunlight. In cold, dry, sunny climates the latent flux reverses, the surface loses mass by sublimation, and on the high tropical and subtropical glaciers this sublimation loss can consume so much of the available energy that little is left for melt, sculpting the surface into the blades of ice called penitentes.

## Albedo and the melt–albedo feedback

The albedo deserves separate treatment because it both controls melt and is controlled by it. Fresh snow is bright because its fine, multiply scattering grains return most of the incident light, the optical behaviour set out in {doc}`../foundations/optical-properties`; as snow ages its grains grow and round, its albedo falls, and once the snow has gone the underlying ice is darker still. Melt therefore lowers the albedo, the lower albedo raises the absorbed shortwave, and the increased absorption drives further melt, a positive feedback that makes the timing of snow disappearance one of the strongest controls on a season's total melt. The feedback is amplified by darkening agents at the surface, the wind-blown dust and the pigmented algae and cryoconite of {doc}`surface-hydrology`, which lower the albedo of bare ice further and have been implicated in the recent darkening of the Greenland ablation zone. Because the feedback turns on sharply when the surface reaches the melting point and the snowpack is exhausted, a small warming can produce a disproportionate increase in melt, which is part of why the surface mass balance of an ice sheet is so sensitive to temperature.

## What the budget produces

The output of the surface energy balance is a melt rate, and that meltwater has only a few fates, each taken up in the chapters that follow. Where the underlying firn is cold, the meltwater percolates and refreezes, releasing its latent heat back into the firn and building the ice lenses and layers that distinguish the facies of {doc}`../foundations/snow-to-ice`; refreezing returns the energy to the glacier and removes none of its mass. Where the firn is already at the melting point or absent, the water runs off, gathering into the supraglacial streams, lakes, and moulins of {doc}`surface-hydrology` and eventually reaching the bed or the ocean. The partition between these fates, refreezing versus runoff, is itself part of the surface mass balance and is one of the harder quantities to model.

For the prognostic forcing of {doc}`../climate/climate-forcing`, the full budget developed here is reduced to a parameterization. The positive-degree-day model replaces the whole balance with a single empirical factor multiplying the temperature, which works because most of the terms covary with air temperature, while the energy-balance model retains the individual fluxes where that approximation fails, on maritime glaciers dominated by turbulent exchange and on surfaces where the albedo feedback is decisive. Both are how the physics of this chapter enters a model that must run for centuries; the chapter itself is the account of where the melt comes from, the surface boundary condition that closes the heat flow begun at the bed in {doc}`thermal-structure`.
