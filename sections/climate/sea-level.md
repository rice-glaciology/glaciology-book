# Sea-level rise and its impacts

The previous chapter ended among the ice shells of other worlds, and it is worth pausing on what it costs to study them. The missions that sounded the polar layered deposits of Mars and that will sound the shell of Europa are flagship efforts, built and flown for billions of dollars each, and they are worth every cent; finding and reading ice across the solar system is one of the things our species does that history will remember. But the comparison with Earth is uncomfortable. The satellites that watch our own ice, the altimeters, the gravity pairs, the radar interferometers of the observing part, have flown as smaller missions that fight for continuation one at a time, and the record they keep has gaps to show for it, a decade-long hole between laser altimeters, a year of darkness between GRACE and its follow-on. We have, at times, held a better-funded view of the ice on Mars than of the ice that sets the coastline of Bangladesh. This final chapter is about the question that asymmetry should sharpen, what Earth's ice is about to do to the ocean, how the machinery of this book is used to answer it, and who is waiting on the answer.

## Ice-sheet contributions to sea level

Spread over the global ocean, about 362 gigatonnes of ice raises mean sea level by one millimetre, so the mass-change time series of {doc}`../observing/gravity` convert directly into sea-level currency. The ice sheets and mountain glaciers together are now adding on the order of a millimetre per year and accelerating, already rivaling the thermal expansion of the warming ocean. The reservoirs set the stakes. Greenland holds about seven metres of sea-level equivalent, West Antarctica's marine basins about five, and the East Antarctic sheet, long treated as inert, several tens of metres, of which even a fractional contribution would dominate everything else. Nothing in this book suggests those reservoirs empty quickly; everything in {doc}`../cryosphere/ice-sheets` suggests the marine portions cannot be counted on to empty slowly once their grounding lines find retrograde slopes.

## Sea-level fingerprints

The single cleanest result in the subject is that the ocean does not rise evenly, and it follows from physics this book already owns. An ice sheet is a mass, and like any mass it pulls the ocean toward itself, so the sea surface stands anomalously high around a large ice sheet. Remove the ice and two things happen at once, the ocean gains water and loses an attractor. Within roughly two thousand kilometres of the melting source, the gravitational relaxation wins and *relative sea level falls*; in the far field, the rise exceeds the global mean by up to a third {cite}`farrell1976,mitrovica2009`. The fingerprints are distinctive enough to act as forensic evidence. Greenland's meltwater largely spares northern Europe and loads the tropical Pacific and Southern Hemisphere; a West Antarctic collapse delivers its worst, about a quarter above the global mean, to the coasts of North America and the Indian Ocean. Add the slow isostatic adjustment of {doc}`../cryosphere/former-glaciers`, by which coastlines around vanished ice sheets are still rising or sinking today, and "how much will sea level rise" becomes a question with a different answer in every harbor, answerable only by carrying the ice-sheet projections of this book through the solid-Earth physics.

## Projection methods and uncertainty

Projection is now an organized, intercompared, community effort. Ensembles such as ISMIP6 run many ice-sheet models, icepack's relatives, under common climate scenarios and publish the spread {cite}`seroussi2020`, and the anatomy of that spread reads like this book's table of contents. Part of it is scenario uncertainty, which emissions path the world takes, and is not glaciology's to reduce. The rest is ours. The choice of friction law, the same choice the lab of {doc}`../thermomechanics/sliding-laws-lab` showed deciding how hard a Thwaites-like glacier responds to thinning, propagates into tens of centimetres of global spread by 2100. The ocean-melt parameterizations of {doc}`climate-forcing` carry comparable weight, and the committed response of {doc}`../modeling/prognostic-problem` guarantees a floor under every scenario, sea-level rise that continues for centuries after warming stops. Beyond the ensembles sits the deep-uncertainty tail, mechanisms such as marine ice-cliff failure that, if real, roughly double Antarctica's plausible century contribution {cite}`deconto2016`; the honest statement is that the upper tail of sea-level projections is set not by likely physics but by physics we cannot yet rule out. Reducing that tail is the clearest practical payoff on offer for every open problem flagged in this book, the friction laws, the damage and calving physics, the observations of the bed we would like to use more.

## Impacts on downstream communities

Downstream of the projections are people, and the water is not headed for them evenly. Hundreds of millions live within two metres of high tide, crowded onto exactly the landforms a rising ocean takes first, the deltas of the Ganges-Brahmaputra, the Mekong, and the Nile, the barrier coasts, the atoll nations, and most of them contributed almost nothing to the forcing that is coming for them. The fingerprint physics sharpens the injustice rather than softening it, because both polar fingerprints run above the global mean across the tropical ocean, which is where most of the exposed population lives. Sea-level rise does not arrive as a tide chart. It arrives as salt in the wells and the rice paddies years before the land itself goes under, as storm surge reaching streets it never reached, as insurance withdrawn, harvests abandoned, and finally as the forced departure of people from places they had every intention of keeping. The Mi'kmaq community of Lennox Island, met in this book's introduction, has already watched a substantial fraction of its land go under within living memory. Tuvalu, Kiribati, and the Marshall Islands are drafting the legal machinery of statehood designed to outlive its own territory, and the projection ranges of this chapter are the numbers that machinery is being built against. Nor is the downstream only coastal. The mountain glaciers of {doc}`glacier-variations` are the dry-season water supply of the Indus, the Andes, and central Asia, and their decline is already pulling that supply out from under hundreds of millions more, while the outburst floods of {doc}`../thermomechanics/hydrology` have killed tens of thousands of people in Peru's Cordillera Blanca alone. None of this is a distant scenario awaiting confirmation; every item on the list is underway, and the projections decide its pace and its endpoint, not whether it happens. When the introduction asked for whom glaciology is done, this is the concrete answer the projection era gives. It is done for the people downstream, who are mostly not the people doing it, and that gap is the discipline's standing critique of itself {cite}`robel2024`.

## Conclusion

This book began with a molecule and ends with a coastline, and the chain between them is unbroken. The bent molecule built an open crystal; the crystal's defects let ice creep; creep plus gravity made glaciers flow; flow carried ice to warm oceans and low elevations where climate takes it apart; and the rate of that dismantling, integrated over this century, will be measured in homes, in harvests, and in the borders of nations. The diagnostic and prognostic problems this book was organized around turn out to be one civic question asked twice, how fast is the ice moving, and where will the water be when our students are old. The answer is not optional and not deferrable, because seawalls are engineered against a number, treaties are negotiated against a number, and the decision to defend a coastline or surrender it is, at bottom, a decision about which end of an error bar to believe. The subject's oldest record is an observation, voyagers in the seventh century reading pack ice off a wooden hull, and its newest deliverable is that number. Between those two acts of attention, observing ice and answering for it, lies everything this book has tried to teach, and the parts we still cannot project, the friction beneath an ice stream, the cliff that may or may not fail, are not footnotes. They are the width of the error bar, the width of the error bar sets the cost of every decision downstream of it, and narrowing it is the most consequential work a reader of this book can choose to do.
