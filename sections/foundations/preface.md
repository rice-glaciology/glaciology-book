# Preface

The opening chapters of this book develop the physics of glacier ice, beginning with the water molecule and proceeding through the structure, vibrations, and defects of the ice crystal. Water is a familiar substance but an unusual one, and many of the properties that matter most in glaciology are anomalies when water is compared with chemically similar compounds. This preface collects the principal anomalies of water, each of which is explained in a later chapter. Fuller treatments are given by {cite}`fletcher1970` and {cite}`petrenko1999`.

## The anomalies of water

Water ice is less dense than the liquid water. The density of ice at 0 °C is 917 kg m⁻³, about nine percent less than that of water, a reversal of the usual order of phases shared by only a handful of substances. Because of it, ice floats, lakes and oceans freeze from the surface downward while life persists in the liquid beneath, ice shelves and sea ice exist at all, and pressure lowers the melting point, a fact of consequence at the bed of a thick ice sheet. The liquid itself has a density maximum near 4 °C, so that cold lakes are stably stratified with their warmest water at depth.

The melting and boiling points of water are high relative to those of molecules of similar molecular weight. Water's chemical relatives melt at much lower temperatures, H₂Te near −49 °C, H₂Se near −66 °C, and H₂S near −86 °C, and extrapolation of this series to the molecular weight of water suggests a melting point near −100 °C; the observed value is 0 °C. Molecules of comparable mass outside the series behave like the heavier hydrides rather than like water, methane melting at −182 °C and ammonia at −78 °C, so water is the only molecule of its size that is solid at the temperatures of the Earth's surface, and the existence of abundant ice on this planet depends on the anomaly.

The heat capacity is also very high relative to other molecules. The heat capacity of liquid water, about 75 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ or nine times the gas constant, is larger than that of any other common liquid; the latent heat of fusion is 334 kJ kg⁻¹, and the latent heat of sublimation is nearly an order of magnitude larger again. These quantities moderate climate, dominate the energy balance of a melting glacier surface, and make latent heat the largest term in most of the budgets written later in the book.

These anomalies have a common origin asociated with the shape of the water molecule and it's orbital structure. They also have cascading effects that affect how ice interacts with other components of the earth-climate system. Water expands on freezing with enough force to fracture rock, which makes it particularly effective at eroding its substrate. Liquid water can be supercooled by tens of degrees before freezing spontaneously, which is why clouds of liquid droplets and ice crystals coexist. Both the liquid and the solid phases of water have unusually large dielectric constants, about 80 (units) for water and about 100 (units) for ice at microwave frequencies, which underlie the electrical properties of ice treated in the defect chapters, and the radar methods of the observing chapters.
