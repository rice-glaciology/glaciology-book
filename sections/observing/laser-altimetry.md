# Laser altimetry

Height is the observable that converts most directly into mass, and the most direct way to measure it from space is to bounce a laser off the ice and time the return. A satellite laser altimeter sends a short pulse of light straight down, records the round-trip travel time, and multiplies by the speed of light to get the range to the surface; combined with the precisely known position of the spacecraft, this gives the surface elevation. Because light reflects from the true snow surface rather than penetrating it, and because a laser beam can be made narrow, laser altimetry gives the cleanest and most precise surface heights available. This chapter describes the method and what it measures. It is the first of the elevation techniques surveyed in {doc}`elevation`.

## The instruments

Two satellite missions define the record. ICESat carried a full-waveform laser from 2003 to 2009, recording the shape of the returned pulse over a footprint of about seventy metres. Its successor ICESat-2, flying since 2018, works differently: rather than measuring one strong pulse it counts individual returned photons, firing a green laser ten thousand times a second and splitting it into six beams in three pairs {cite}`markus2017`. Each beam has a footprint of only about eleven metres, the pairs straddle the track to measure across-track slope, and the dense stream of photons builds a precise profile of elevation along the ground track with a vertical precision of centimetres. Between the two missions, airborne lidar flown during Operation IceBridge bridged the gap in time and provided higher-resolution surveys of targeted areas. The one limitation common to all laser altimeters is cloud, which blocks the beam, so the coverage is dense along-track but interrupted in time.

## From photons to elevation change

The raw measurement from ICESat-2 is a cloud of individual photons, most of them solar background rather than signal, scattered in time around the true surface return. The first processing step is to find the surface within that cloud, separating signal photons from noise and fitting a height to short along-track segments, the product distributed as along-track land-ice height. Corrections are then applied for the solid-earth and ocean tides, for the small delay the pulse suffers passing through the atmosphere, and for the saturation of the detector over bright surfaces. The science comes from repeating the measurement. Because ICESat-2 flies repeat ground tracks, differencing the height of the same track between two cycles, or comparing crossing tracks, gives the rate of surface elevation change, and tying ICESat-2 back to ICESat extends the record across a decade and a half. A worked data-processing lab for this chapter starts from the along-track height product and recovers an elevation-change rate.

## What it reveals

Surface elevation is the geometry that flow enters through, the thickness and the surface slope that set the driving stress of {doc}`../ice_flow/stress-balance`, and its change through time is the clearest signal of an ice sheet out of balance. The laser record has mapped the rapid lowering of the Amundsen Sea glaciers in West Antarctica, where the surface is dropping by metres a year as the ice accelerates, against the near-balance of the high interior. Over floating ice the same heights become thickness, because an ice shelf floats in hydrostatic balance, so a measured freeboard converts to a thickness once the firn air content of {doc}`../foundations/snow-to-ice` is accounted for. Turning a surface-height change into a mass change requires the same firn correction, since part of any height change is low-density firn rather than solid ice. The dense, precise profiles also resolve the depth of supraglacial lakes, the flexure of ice in the grounding zone under the ocean tide, and the freeboard of sea ice, making the laser altimeter one of the most versatile instruments in the observing system.
