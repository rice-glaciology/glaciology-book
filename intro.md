# Introduction to Glaciology and Ice-Flow Modeling

Welcome to the Rice Glaciology group's open textbook. Here, we introduce the physics of glaciers and ice sheets, demonstrate how the physics of ice is encoded in numerical ice flow models like [icepack](https://icepack.github.io/), and walk through several examples of observations of the crosphere that are actively shaping our understanding of Earth's ice sheets.

The book is aimed at advanced undergraduates and beginning graduate students who have taken some calculus and physics, but who are new to glaciology. We build up the ideas incrementally and try to introduce mathematics and physics using notebooks intended to gamify learning. Much of the material that we present borrows from standard references like *The Physics of Glaciers* {cite}`cuffey2010`, and *Physics of ice* {cite}'Petrenko'; we point to specific chapters in these texts and other references for readers who want to dive deeper.

## First observers

The scientific study of ice is relatively young (maybe a reference here of when some of the first experinets on ice were conducted by Pauling), but observations of glaciers and ice sheets are not. In the oral histories of the Māori tribal groups Ngāti Rārua and Te Āti Awa, the navigator Hui Te Rangiora sailed south from the Cook Islands on the vessel Te Ivi o Atea around 650 CE, into waters his crew described as *Te tai-uka-a-pia*, the sea foaming like arrowroot, the scraped white powder of pia being the closest thing in a tropical Pacific world to the appearance of floe ice {cite}`wehi2021`. The accounts speak of a white land that was floating, of bare summits piercing the sky, of animals that dive to great depths. These are likely some of the first observations of Antarctic ice shelves, and the marine life of the Southern Ocean, made and preserved roughly twelve centuries before the continent entered European charts. Ngāi Tahu and other iwi hold these voyaging histories among their treasured knowledge; a carved pou by the Ngāi Tahu artist Fayne Robinson, Te Kaiwhakatere o te Taki, the Navigator of the Heavens, has stood at Scott Base since 2013 with its face turned to the sky that guided those voyages; and Māori scholars today argue for an Antarctic future managed with Indigenous frameworks alongside the science {cite}`wehi2021policy`.

Sámi reindeer herders in northern Fennoscandia also maintain a taxonomy of snow far finer than anything in this book, dozens of named types distinguished by the density, hardness, and layering that decide whether animals can dig to forage. Communities in the Andes, the Himalaya, Alaska, and Greenland have watched, named, and depended on their glaciers for centuries. The instrumental record that this book leans on is the newest layer in a long human archive of ice, and it is worth knowing that the equations are not where the subject began.

## For whom, and by whom?

It is fair to ask of any science who it serves and who gets to do it {cite}`robel2024`. Glaciological inquiry in the academy grew out of early expeditions from nineteenth-century Europe that continue to shape research agendas and access to the continent. Polar field sciences built a heroic self-image led almost exclusively by white men and mythologized as the conquest of hostile places. That image hardened into a gatekeeping conflation of scientific ability with outdoorsmanship that has no basis in the physics; as late as 2009, fewer than a fifth of the authors in the field's flagship journals were women, and a feminist analysis of the field's history and framing landed only in the last decade {cite}`carey2016`. 

The question of "for whom" has an even sharper present tense. The people most exposed to the cryosphere's changes are disproportionately Indigenous and are rarely at the table where the science is framed and shared. Demographic surveys of major polar collaborations remain overwhelmingly white {cite}`robel2024`. We keep this question open through the book. It has informed and motivated the writing. The tools here are deliberately open, and the content editable as part of a philosophy that embraces progressive evaluation of the science, science-history and understanding.

## How the book is organized

The chapters of our book build on oneanother and are ordered around the ingredients needed to solve equations that describe ice flow. We first develop fundmental understanding of the water molecule and polycrystalline ice. This discussion builds to the formulation of momentum balance equations in ice flow models and the diagnostic problem of calculating the velocity of a glacier from the glaicer's geometry. We then explore climate and model forcing, and identfy the prognostic problem of evolving glacier geometry forward in time. Labs run throughout the whole book connecting these topics and applying mechanics to real world glaciers.

A short **getting started** chapter introduces icepack, the Python package for glacier and ice-sheet flow modeling built on the finite element library [Firedrake](https://www.firedrakeproject.org/) {cite}`shapero2021`, and the Docker image that runs it. The **physics of ice** chapter then builds the physical understanding of ice starting from the water molecule. It is more chemistry than a flow modeler strictly needs, but it pays off because the the rheology of ice (how it flows) is most honestly understood from the crystal scale processes that control ice motion. The **Ice deformation and flow** chapter develops the concepts ot stress and strain, the implications of ice's nonlinear rheology, the momentum balance and its hierarchy of approximations, and ends by solving the diagnostic problem with icepack on a synthetic ice shelf. **The bed and basal processes** chapter introduces heat flow, which controls whether the bed is frozen or thawed, develops the mechanics of glacier sliding, and concludes with a lab comparing friction laws on a Thwaites-like glacier.

The prognostic half begins with **mass balance and climate**, accumulation and ablation, firn, glacier response theory, the ice sheets and their instabilities, and the climate archive in the ice. Firn is deliberately held here rather than placed with the physics of ice at the front; it belongs to the surface and to climate, it records past atmospheres in ice cores, and it complicates the conversion of satellite elevation change into mass change. **Prognostic modeling and climate forcing** then makes the evolution problem concrete, the thickness equation as a nonlinear diffusion marched forward in time, the practical hundred-to-thousand-year horizon of such experiments, and the reduced models that carry the story beyond that horizon, opening from the recognition that on long timescales ice sheets are not driven by climate so much as they are a part of it. **Observing ice** follows, framed around the products that ice-flow models consume, velocities for inversions, elevations for mass balance, bed geometry from radar, and equally around the observations we would like to use more, the seismicity, electrical soundings, and phase-sensitive radar that sense processes at the bed we still do not understand well enough to model. The part works from the raw data up: where the modeling chapters used finished datasets, these chapters and their labs process actual measurements, photon clouds, waveforms, interferograms, seismograms, into those datasets, so the products stop being ground truth and become measurements with assumptions. **The wider cryosphere** closes the book with sea ice, the landscapes of former glaciers, permafrost, the ice of other worlds, where every tool built here is needed again, and finally sea level itself, the projection problem that carries everything in this book downstream to the people waiting on the answer.

Heat flow sits across the divide between the two problems. The temperature field evolves with the flow, which makes it prognostic, yet its quasi-steady consequence at the bed is exactly the boundary condition the diagnostic problem needs. We introduce it with the basal processes for that reason, and return to its full time evolution, coupled to climate, with the prognostic material.

## Running the code

The modeling chapters use icepack, which runs on Firedrake. Firedrake can be awkward to install directly, so this book ships a **Docker image** that gives you a ready-to-go environment with Firedrake, icepack, and JupyterLab. See {doc}`sections/modeling/running-icepack-docker`. Because that stack is heavy, the notebooks are **not executed** when the book is built — you run them yourself in the container.
