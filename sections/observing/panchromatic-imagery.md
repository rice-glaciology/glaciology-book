# Panchromatic imagery

A photograph of an ice sheet seems the most ordinary of observations, but high-resolution optical imagery has become one of the most powerful tools in glaciology, because the same pictures yield both the shape of the ice and its motion. The images are panchromatic, recorded in a single broad band across the visible spectrum rather than split into colours, which lets each pixel collect more light and so gives the sharpest possible resolution, a few tens of centimetres on the ground from a commercial satellite. From overlapping panchromatic images one reconstructs surface elevation by stereo photogrammetry, and from repeated images one measures surface velocity by tracking features as they move. This chapter covers both, completing the surface-geometry methods of {doc}`elevation` and complementing the radar interferometry of {doc}`insar`.

## Elevation from stereo

Two images of the same surface taken from slightly different viewing angles contain, in the parallax between them, the third dimension, exactly as the offset between a person's two eyes gives depth. Matching the images feature by feature and solving the geometry recovers the surface height at every point, producing a digital elevation model at a resolution of a few metres. Applied systematically to commercial satellite imagery, this has produced the continental mosaics that now underpin the field: the Reference Elevation Model of Antarctica and its Arctic counterpart give a detailed, nearly complete snapshot of the ice-sheet surface {cite}`howat2019`. Differencing two such elevation models built from imagery taken at different times gives the elevation change directly, once they are carefully co-registered to remove the small horizontal and vertical offsets between them, and this is how thinning is mapped over the rugged outlet glaciers that altimeters resolve poorly.

## Velocity from feature tracking

The second use needs no stereo. If a glacier carries crevasses, rifts, or patches of dirty ice, those features move with the flow, and cross-correlating a small image patch from one date against a later image finds the offset that best aligns them, the displacement of the surface over the interval {cite}`scambos1992`. Dividing by the time between images gives the velocity, and repeating the correlation across the whole scene gives a velocity map. The method works wherever the surface has trackable texture and the features survive between acquisitions, which makes it especially good on fast, crevassed outlet glaciers, the same places where the phase coherence that radar interferometry relies on tends to break down. A data-processing lab for this chapter cross-correlates two image patches to recover a displacement, the kernel of both feature tracking and the speckle tracking used with radar.

## What it reveals

The reach of optical imagery in time is remarkable. Declassified reconnaissance photographs from the 1960s, processed with the same stereo and tracking methods, extend the record of elevation and velocity back six decades, long before the satellite era proper. In the present the imagery resolves the growth and drainage of supraglacial lakes, the propagation of crevasses and rifts toward a calving event, the precise position of calving fronts through time, and the velocity of glaciers that are changing too fast for slower methods to follow. The elevation models feed the geometry and surface slope that drive the flow in {doc}`../ice_flow/stress-balance`, and the velocity fields feed the diagnostic inversions for the bed in {doc}`../thermomechanics/basal-motion`, so a single archive of pictures supplies two of the three things a flow model most needs.
