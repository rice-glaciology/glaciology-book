# Surface hydrology

The subglacial drainage of {doc}`hydrology` was developed from its outlet, the effective pressure it delivers to the bed, but the water it carries has to arrive from somewhere, and on most glaciers it arrives from the surface. This chapter follows the meltwater from the moment it is produced at the surface to the moment it reaches the bed or leaves the glacier, the supraglacial and englacial part of the plumbing that the subglacial chapter took as a given input. The route matters because the timing and the volume of water delivered to the bed control the seasonal acceleration of the ice, because surface lakes can open fractures that drain to the bed in hours, and because meltwater that refreezes in the firn warms it and changes its density. The treatment follows the water-flow chapter of {cite}`cuffey2010`.

## Surface melt and the weathering crust

Surface melt is produced by the energy balance at the ice surface developed in {doc}`surface-energy-balance`. That chapter decides how much water is made; this one follows what becomes of it once it exists.

The first thing that happens is that some of it is stored close to where it formed. Solar radiation penetrates the upper few tens of centimetres of ice and melts preferentially at grain boundaries below the surface, producing a porous, water-saturated layer called the weathering crust. The crust acts as a shallow aquifer that buffers the diurnal melt cycle, releasing water slowly through the day and into the night, and it is the reason a glacier surface continues to discharge water for hours after the sun has gone off it. Scattered across the ablation zone are cryoconite holes, small water-filled cylinders melted into the ice by dark wind-blown dust and microbial material whose low albedo absorbs sunlight, each a few centimetres across and a few centimetres deep. The crust and the cryoconite holes are minor reservoirs individually, but together they govern how quickly the surface responds to a change in melt and how much of the surface darkens, the albedo feedback that itself amplifies melt.

## Supraglacial streams and lakes

Water that is not stored locally runs downslope and organizes itself into a drainage network on the ice surface. Supraglacial streams incise channels into the ice and gather into rivers that can be metres deep and tens of kilometres long, draining catchments that are as sharply defined on the ice surface as river basins are on land. On the southwest Greenland ablation zone these supraglacial rivers carry the great majority of the surface melt, and their networks deliver it to the interior of the ice sheet far more efficiently than diffuse percolation could {cite}`smith2015`. Most such catchments are internally drained, ending not at the ice margin but at a moulin or a crevasse field that swallows the stream.

Where the surface slope is gentle and a hollow in the ice traps water faster than it can drain, a supraglacial lake forms. The hollows are set by the bed topography transmitted through the ice, so the same lakes refill in the same basins year after year as the melt season advances. Lakes are the most visible feature of the summer ice-sheet surface, kilometres across and several metres deep, and their dark water absorbs far more solar radiation than the surrounding ice, both deepening themselves and warming the water they hold. A lake represents a large, concentrated mass of water perched on the ice, and how it drains is the central question of surface hydrology.

## Hydrofracture and the drainage of lakes

A lake can drain slowly, by overtopping its rim and cutting an outlet channel, or it can drain catastrophically, by opening a fracture straight through the ice sheet beneath it. The catastrophic route is hydrofracture. A water-filled crack differs from a dry one because the water pressure pushes the crack walls apart, and once the crack is deep enough the pressure of the water column exceeds the compressive stress that would otherwise hold the crack shut. The condition is captured by comparing the density of water with that of ice. A dry crevasse in ice penetrates only to a depth of about thirty metres, where the inward squeeze of the ice closes it, but a crack filled with water to its top can in principle propagate to any depth, because water is denser than ice and the pressure at the crack tip keeps rising faster than the ice can resist {cite}`vanderveen2007`. A lake holding a few metres of water over a crevasse field supplies exactly this condition.

The consequences were measured directly in Greenland, where a supraglacial lake of several million cubic metres drained to the bed through nearly a kilometre of ice in under two hours, faster than Niagara Falls, lifting and accelerating the surrounding ice as the water reached the bed and pressurized it {cite}`das2008`. The event delivered not only water but heat, the latent and potential energy of the falling water warming the ice around the new conduit. On floating ice the same physics turns mechanical. The flexural stresses set up as lakes fill and drain across an ice shelf can propagate fractures through the shelf itself, and a chain reaction of draining lakes is the mechanism proposed for the sudden disintegration of the Larsen B Ice Shelf {cite}`banwell2013`, the connection between surface water and ice-shelf collapse taken up in {doc}`../cryosphere/instabilities` and {doc}`../climate/sea-level`.

## Moulins and the englacial system

Most surface water reaches the bed not through a single dramatic lake drainage but steadily, through moulins. A moulin is a vertical shaft kept open by the stream that pours into it, melted and wedged through the ice along a crevasse, and it is the standing connection between the surface network and the interior of the glacier. Below the moulin the water travels through an englacial network of conduits to the bed, where it joins the subglacial system of {doc}`hydrology`. The englacial passage introduces a delay and a storage term between surface melt and basal discharge, so the water pressure at the bed responds to the surface melt smoothed and lagged rather than instantly, and a moulin that feeds a subglacial channel buffers the diurnal melt cycle into a steadier basal supply. The supply that the subglacial chapter assumed at its upstream end is, in detail, the output of this englacial routing.

## Refreezing, firn aquifers, and retained meltwater

Not all surface melt runs off. In the percolation and wet-snow facies of {doc}`../foundations/snow-to-ice`, meltwater sinks into cold firn and refreezes, the metamorphism developed in {doc}`../foundations/snow-processes`. Refreezing is a powerful brake on runoff, since a cold firn column can absorb a season's melt without losing any mass to the ocean, and it is the reason the surface mass balance of an ice sheet is not simply accumulation minus melt. The latent heat it releases also matters for the energy budget, since it can raise the temperature of the firn by many degrees and bring a cold near-surface layer to the melting point sooner than conduction alone would.

Where melt is abundant and the overlying winter snowfall is thick enough to insulate it, water can survive the winter without refreezing, forming a perennial firn aquifer, a body of liquid water held in the pore space of the firn year-round. Such aquifers were found to be extensive in the high-accumulation wet-snow zones of southeast Greenland, storing a volume of water comparable to a year's surface melt and draining slowly toward the margins {cite}`forster2014`. The firn aquifer is a slow reservoir in the same system the rest of this chapter describes, and it complicates the interpretation of radar and altimetry over the percolation zone, since liquid water in the firn changes both its density and its dielectric properties.

## Surface melt and ice dynamics

The reason surface hydrology belongs in a book about ice flow is that the water it delivers to the bed changes how fast the ice moves. When surface melt reaches a subglacial system that has not yet developed efficient channels, the water pressure rises, the effective pressure falls, and through the friction law of {doc}`basal-motion` the ice accelerates, the seasonal speed-up first attributed to surface melt on the Greenland Ice Sheet by {cite}`zwally2002`. As the melt season proceeds the subglacial channels of {doc}`hydrology` grow, the bed drains, and the ice slows again even as melting continues, so the relationship between annual melt and annual ice motion is not simply that more melt means faster flow. A self-organizing drainage system can accommodate a larger steady supply by channelizing more completely, which is why the long-term sensitivity of ice-sheet flow to increasing surface melt remains an active question rather than a settled one.

The thread of this chapter is that the surface is the source and the bed is the sink, with the firn, the streams, the lakes, the moulins, and the englacial conduits as the stages in between. Each stage stores water and releases it on its own timescale, so the basal supply that drives the sliding of {doc}`basal-motion` and the erosion of {doc}`../cryosphere/erosion` is the surface melt of {doc}`../climate/climate-forcing` filtered through the whole of the system described here.
