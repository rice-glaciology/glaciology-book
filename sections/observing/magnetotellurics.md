# Magnetotellurics

Whether a glacier slides depends on what lies at its bed, and one of the most telling properties of that material is its electrical resistivity. Cold ice is an excellent insulator, clean bedrock is resistive, but liquid water is conductive, and salty water and water-saturated clay-rich sediment are very conductive indeed. A method that maps resistivity beneath the ice therefore maps the water and sediment that the friction laws of {doc}`../thermomechanics/basal-motion` and the drainage of {doc}`../thermomechanics/hydrology` depend on. Magnetotellurics is that method. It uses the natural electromagnetic fields of the Earth as its source, needs no transmitter, and reaches from the surface of the ice down through the bed into the crust beneath. This chapter describes it, drawing on the same electromagnetic response of ice developed in {doc}`../radar/em-waves` but at frequencies a million times lower.

## Natural fields as a sounding source

The Earth is bathed in naturally varying magnetic fields, generated by currents in the magnetosphere and by lightning around the world, spanning a wide range of frequencies. These fields induce electric currents in the conducting ground, and the relationship between the horizontal electric and magnetic fields measured at the surface, the impedance, carries the resistivity of the material below. The key is the skin effect: a field of a given frequency penetrates to a depth that grows as the resistivity rises and as the frequency falls, so low frequencies sound deep and high frequencies sound shallow. Measuring the impedance across a band of frequencies therefore returns the resistivity as a function of depth, a sounding from the surface down through the ice and into whatever lies beneath, with no need to put any energy into the ground.

## From time series to a resistivity model

A magnetotelluric station records the orthogonal components of the electric and magnetic fields over hours to days. The processing transforms these time series into the frequency domain and forms the impedance tensor at each frequency, the transfer function between the electric and magnetic fields, from which apparent resistivity and a phase are derived as functions of period. A thick resistive ice layer sits at the top of every Antarctic sounding and must be accounted for before the structure beneath can be read. The apparent-resistivity curves from one or many stations are then inverted for a model of resistivity against depth and position, in one, two, or three dimensions. A data-processing lab for this chapter estimates apparent resistivity and phase from a pair of electric and magnetic time series, the first step of any magnetotelluric survey.

## What it reveals

The method came of age in glaciology when it was used to look beneath an ice stream. A magnetotelluric survey across Whillans Ice Stream revealed a deep, conductive layer interpreted as a vast reservoir of saline groundwater in the sediments below the ice, a body of water far larger than the thin film at the bed and one that exchanges with it, reshaping the picture of the subglacial water budget that the hydrology chapter builds {cite}`gustafson2022`. Elsewhere magnetotellurics has mapped the thickness of subglacial sediment, distinguished frozen from thawed ground, and traced the conductive pathways of subglacial water. Because it sees exactly the water and sediment that control sliding but are nearly invisible to radar, magnetotellurics has become a key tool for characterizing the bed, complementing the seismology of {doc}`cryoseismicity` that senses the same bed by its mechanics.
