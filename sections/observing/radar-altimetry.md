# Radar altimetry

Swap the laser's light for microwaves and the altimeter gains one great advantage at the price of one characteristic difficulty. The advantage is that microwaves pass through cloud, so a radar altimeter observes in all weather, day and night, and has built an unbroken record of ice-sheet elevation stretching back to the early 1990s. The difficulty is that microwaves also pass a little way into dry snow before they scatter, so the echo does not come cleanly from the surface, and extracting a height demands more care than a laser return does. This chapter covers the method; it follows the laser altimetry of {doc}`laser-altimetry` and the dielectric behaviour of ice in {doc}`../radar/em-waves`, which is why the radar penetrates the snow at all.

## The instruments and the footprint problem

The European missions ERS-1, ERS-2, and Envisat carried conventional pulse-limited altimeters whose footprint on the surface is large, several kilometres across, which blurs the steep and rapidly changing margins where the ice matters most. CryoSat-2, launched in 2010 to address exactly this, carries a radar that can operate in a synthetic-aperture mode, sharpening the footprint along-track by processing the Doppler history of the echoes, and in an interferometric mode with two antennas that measures the across-track angle to the reflecting point and so locates returns from sloping surfaces {cite}`wingham2006`. RadarSat and other synthetic-aperture imaging radars contribute surface elevation in a different way, through the interferometry and DEM mosaics of {doc}`insar` and {doc}`panchromatic-imagery` rather than through ranging, but the satellite radar altimeter remains the workhorse for monitoring the slow change of ice-sheet volume over decades.

## Retracking and the penetration correction

The returned waveform of a radar altimeter is a curve, the echo power spread out in time, and the surface height is encoded in where the leading edge of that curve rises. Finding that point, called retracking, is the central processing step, and different retracking algorithms make different assumptions about the shape of the echo. Two corrections then matter especially over ice. A slope correction accounts for the echo arriving from the nearest point on a tilted surface rather than from directly below, and a penetration correction accounts for the part of the echo that comes from scattering within the snow rather than from the surface, a bias that shifts as the snow wets, refreezes, or changes density between seasons and years. These corrections are the main source of uncertainty in radar-altimeter elevation change, and a data-processing lab for this chapter retracks a waveform and forms a basin-scale elevation-change time series to show how they enter.

## Glaciological applications

Despite its complications the radar record is long and continuous, and it is the backbone of the long-term accounting of ice-sheet volume change. It showed the early thinning of the Amundsen Sea sector, tracks the slow elevation changes of the interiors that reflect shifting snowfall, and over sea ice it measures freeboard and thence thickness. Like the laser, it gives the geometry that drives the flow and, with a firn densification model from {doc}`../foundations/snow-to-ice`, the conversion of volume change into mass change. The two altimeters are complementary: the laser is precise and clean but interrupted by cloud, the radar is continuous and all-weather but biased by penetration, and the modern record of ice-sheet change rests on reconciling them.
