# Energy levels in atoms and molecules

The previous chapter assigned energies to bonds and counted electrons into shells without saying where the energies come from or why the shells have the capacities they do. The mechanics behind both is quantum mechanics, and there is no classical substitute, since a classical electron circling a nucleus would radiate away its energy and spiral inward in a few hundredths of a nanosecond, and a classical lattice would have the wrong heat capacity at every temperature for the reasons previewed in the {doc}`preface`. This chapter assembles the quantum ideas the book actually uses, why energies come in discrete levels at all, what orbitals are and why shells fill the way they do, how a molecule's internal states arrange themselves into a ladder of electronic, vibrational, and rotational rungs, and how comparing the spacing of those rungs to $k_B T$ decides where classical reasoning can be trusted. Nothing here requires solving an equation; a handful of estimates and one scaling rule carry all the weight the later chapters place on this material.

## Standing waves and discrete energies

Matter on the atomic scale propagates as waves, with a wavelength tied to momentum by $\lambda = h/p$, where $h$ is Planck's constant. The constant is so small that the wavelength of anything human-sized is absurdly short, some twenty-five orders of magnitude below the size of an atom for a thrown snowball, which is why the wave character of matter is invisible in ordinary life and why classical mechanics serves glaciology perfectly well from the crystal upward. For an electron carrying chemical energies the story inverts, since its wavelength comes out near six angstroms, larger than the molecule that confines it. An electron in a molecule is not a small charged ball in orbit but a wave filling the entire region available to it, and everything in this chapter follows from taking that sentence literally.

A confined wave cannot vibrate at just any frequency. A guitar string of length $L$ sustains only the vibrations that fit a whole number of half-wavelengths between its fixed ends, and an electron wave held in a molecule-sized region is restricted in exactly the same way, to a discrete set of standing-wave patterns, each with a definite energy. Energy levels are not an extra postulate laid on top of the wave picture; they are what confinement does to any wave.

A particle of mass $m$ confined to a region of size $L$ has a lowest energy near $h^2/8mL^2$, and for an electron held in a region the size of a water molecule, three angstroms or so, this comes to about four electron volts, the scale at the top of the previous chapter's ladder of bonds. Chemical energies are electron volts because Planck's constant, the electron mass, and the size of a molecule conspire to make them so. The formula repays attention to its denominator, since the level spacing shrinks as the box grows and as the particle gets heavier, which is the single scaling rule organizing everything below. It explains why the proton, eighteen hundred times heavier than the electron, lives much closer to the classical world, and why energy levels blur toward a classical continuum for large and heavy systems. The formula also forbids rest. A wave with zero energy would be no wave at all, so a confined particle always retains motion in its lowest level, and this irreducible jiggle is the zero-point energy met in the {doc}`preface`, the quantity whose dependence on mass drives the isotopic fractionation at the heart of {doc}`../climate/paleoclimate`.

## Orbitals and the exclusion principle

The standing-wave patterns available to an electron in the electric field of a nucleus are called orbitals. An orbital is not a trajectory but a stationary pattern of electron density with a definite energy, and the patterns come in a hierarchy of shapes, spherical ones labeled $s$, two-lobed ones labeled $p$ pointing along three perpendicular axes, and more elaborate ones beyond that the atoms of ice never need. To the geometry quantum mechanics adds a rule with no classical analogue. Electrons carry an intrinsic two-valued property called spin, and the exclusion principle forbids any two electrons from occupying the same state, so each orbital accommodates at most two electrons of opposite spin. Shell capacities follow by bookkeeping. The first shell consists of the single $1s$ orbital and holds two electrons; the second consists of $2s$ and the three $2p$ orbitals and holds eight. The counting to eight that fixed the formula of water in {doc}`composition` is the exclusion principle at work, and nothing else.

The same wave picture explains what a covalent bond is. When two atoms approach, an electron's standing wave can spread itself across both nuclei at once, and a wave in a bigger box has lower energy by the $1/L^2$ rule above. The energy released by that delocalization is the bond, roughly 460 kJ/mol when the sharing happens between oxygen and hydrogen, and the shared-pair language of the previous chapter is shorthand for two electrons of opposite spin occupying one such molecule-spanning pattern. Which patterns form in water, how the oxygen orbitals rearrange into hybrids, and why two pairs of electrons end up pointed away from the protons as lone pairs are questions taken up in the next chapter, where they fix the geometry on which all of ice depends.

## The energy ladder of a molecule

The ladder of the previous chapter ranked the bonds between particles. A molecule also carries a ladder of states within itself, and its rungs sort into three families separated by orders of magnitude in energy {cite}`herzberg1945`. At the top sit electronic excitations, rearrangements of the electron cloud itself, costing several electron volts; the lowest in water lies near 7 eV, in the far ultraviolet, which is why pure water and clear ice transmit visible light essentially untouched and why their faint blue, treated in {doc}`optical-properties`, must come from somewhere else. In the middle sit vibrations of the nuclei within the molecule. Water has three such modes, a bending of the H–O–H angle and a symmetric and an asymmetric stretching of the two bonds, with quanta of 0.20 to 0.47 eV, and dividing those quanta by $k_B$ gives characteristic temperatures of 2,300 to 5,400 K, the numbers quoted in the {doc}`preface` as the reason the vibrations sit out terrestrial thermodynamics. Quanta of this size are infrared, and these three modes and their crystalline descendants are why ice absorbs strongly across the infrared and why water vapor dominates the atmosphere's infrared opacity. At the bottom sit rotations of the molecule as a whole, with quanta of only a few thousandths of an electron volt, in the far infrared and microwave; in the vapor the molecule tumbles freely on these rungs, while in the crystal free rotation is abolished and replaced by hindered rocking librations whose fate is the business of {doc}`lattice-dynamics`.

A photon of frequency $\nu$ carries energy $h\nu$ and can be absorbed only by a transition of matching spacing, which is why every substance has a spectral fingerprint and why the transparency of a material is as informative as its absorption. Radar photons, at radio frequencies, carry energies ten thousand times smaller than even the rotational rungs, so cold ice offers them no quantum transition to excite and lets them pass through kilometers of glacier, the fact on which the radar methods of the observing part stand. The weak absorption that remains is not a quantum transition at all but the slow classical reorientation of molecules by way of the defects of {doc}`point-defects`, and its dependence on temperature and impurities is what radar attenuation measures.

## Classical and quantum degrees of freedom

One comparison decides where the quantum structure of the ladder matters, the rung spacing against the thermal budget $k_B T$, about a fortieth of an electron volt at the melting point. When the spacing is far below $k_B T$, thermal agitation hops the system freely up and down a ladder so fine it might as well be a ramp, the classical picture holds, and equipartition counts the degree of freedom at its full $\tfrac{1}{2}k_B T$; water's rotations, with their few-meV rungs, are in this regime at terrestrial temperatures, which is why the {doc}`preface` could count them classically in the $4R$ heat capacity. When the spacing is far above $k_B T$, the Boltzmann factor $e^{-h\nu/k_B T}$ strangles excitation, the degree of freedom freezes out, and it vanishes from the heat capacity; water's vibrations, at five to twenty times $k_B T$ per quantum, are in this regime, which is why they are missing from the $4R$ count. Electronic states, at a hundred times $k_B T$ and more, are frozen so completely that every molecule in every glacier on Earth sits in its electronic ground state, and this is a quiet but load-bearing fact, since it means the interactions between molecules can be described by a single potential-energy landscape, the one the next two chapters map. Applied rung by rung to the vibrations of the crystal rather than the molecule, the same comparison will produce the heat capacity of ice itself in {doc}`lattice-dynamics`.

A wave confined by a barrier also refuses to be entirely confined by it. The wave's amplitude leaks into and through any barrier of finite height and width, giving the particle a probability of appearing on the far side that falls off exponentially with the barrier's width and with the square root of the particle's mass. This is tunneling, and its strong mass dependence repeats the chapter's refrain, electrons tunnel promiscuously, protons reluctantly but measurably, deuterons less still. The possibility of proton tunneling shadows every discussion of how protons rearrange within ice, and it resurfaces with the defects of {doc}`point-defects`.

```{admonition} Exercise — confinement energies
:class: tip
The lowest energy of a particle of mass $m$ confined to a region of size $L$ is approximately $E_1 = h^2/8mL^2$.

**(a)** Evaluate $E_1$ for an electron confined to $L = 0.30$ nm, the size of a water molecule, and compare it with the O–H bond energy of about 4.8 eV.

**(b)** Evaluate $E_1$ for a proton in the same region and compare it with $k_B T$ at the melting point, about $1/40$ eV. What does the comparison say about how classically the molecule's nuclei behave?

**(c)** The measured quantum of the O–H stretching vibration is about 0.45 eV. What confinement size $L$ would give a proton this energy, and what does the answer say about how tightly the covalent bond holds its proton?
```

```{admonition} Solution
:class: dropdown
**(a)** $E_1 = h^2/8m_eL^2 = (6.63\times10^{-34})^2 / (8 \times 9.11\times10^{-31} \times (3.0\times10^{-10})^2) \approx 6.7\times10^{-19}$ J $\approx 4.2$ eV. A molecule-sized box gives an electron an energy on the scale of the covalent bond, which is no accident; bond energies are confinement energies.

**(b)** The proton is $1836$ times heavier, so $E_1 \approx 4.2\ \mathrm{eV}/1836 \approx 2.3$ meV, about a tenth of $k_B T$. Confinement at the scale of the whole molecule costs a proton less than thermal energy, so the nuclei are nearly classical residents of the energy landscape the electrons build. The electrons are irreducibly quantum; the nuclei, only marginally so.

**(c)** Solving $E_1 = 0.45$ eV for $L$ with the proton mass gives $L \approx 0.02$ nm, a fifth of an angstrom. The covalent bond confines its proton over ten times more tightly than the size of the molecule, which is why vibrational quanta are large enough to freeze out even though the proton is heavy. Replacing the proton by a deuteron doubles $m$ and lowers the vibrational quantum by $\sqrt{2}$, the mass dependence that the isotope thermometer of {doc}`water-molecule` and {doc}`../climate/paleoclimate` runs on.
```

Energies come in levels because confined waves come in patterns; shells fill because the exclusion principle limits each pattern to two electrons; molecules carry a three-tier ladder of electronic, vibrational, and rotational states; and a single comparison against $k_B T$ sorts every degree of freedom into classical, frozen, or in between. The next chapter brings all of it to bear on one small bent molecule.
