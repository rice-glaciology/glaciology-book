# The chemical building blocks

This chapter describes the background chemistry that the rest of the book draws on, the structure of the atom, the isotopes of hydrogen and oxygen, the types and strengths of chemical bonds, and the impurities and trapped gases present in natural ice. A reader with a recent chemistry background may pass over this material.

## Atoms and elements

An atom consists of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons. The nucleus contains protons, each carrying one unit of positive charge, and electrically neutral neutrons of nearly the same mass. The number of protons, the atomic number, determines the number of electrons in the neutral atom and hence its chemical behavior. Ice is built from hydrogen, atomic number 1, the simplest and most abundant element in the universe, and oxygen, atomic number 8, the third most abundant in the universe and the most abundant in the Earth's crust. The electrons account for less than 0.03 percent of the mass of a water molecule, so the mass of ice is almost entirely comprised in the nuclei, while its chemical and mechanical behavior is determined by the electrons.

Quantities of atoms are counted in moles. One mole is $6.022\times10^{23}$ particles, the number chosen so that one mole of a substance has a mass in grams equal to its molecular weight; one mole of water is 18 g. The size of this number is the reason the statistical methods of the preface succeed, since any measurable quantity of ice contains so many molecules that average behavior is, for all practical purposes, exact.

## Valence and the formula of water

Electrons around a nucleus occupy orbitals grouped into shells of definite capacity, two in the first shell and eight in the second, for reasons set out in {doc}`energy-levels`, and atoms tend to form bonds until their outermost shells are filled. Hydrogen has one electron in a shell that holds two, and accordingly forms one covalent bond, in which two atoms complete their shells by sharing a pair of electrons. Oxygen has six electrons in a shell that holds eight, and forms two covalent bonds. The formula H₂O follows directly, as do the related species that appear in later chapters, the OH radical with one bond unsatisfied, and the ions H₃O⁺ and OH⁻ formed when a proton transfers between molecules. The geometry of the molecule and the disposition of its remaining electron pairs are treated in {doc}`water-molecule`.

## Isotopes

Atoms of a single element may differ in their number of neutrons; such varieties are isotopes, nearly identical in chemistry but different in mass. Hydrogen occurs as ¹H and as deuterium, ²H or D, which carries one neutron; about one hydrogen atom in 6,400 in natural water is deuterium. Oxygen occurs as ¹⁶O together with about 0.2 percent ¹⁸O and a smaller amount of ¹⁷O. Natural ice therefore contains the molecules HDO and H₂¹⁸O in small but measurable proportions alongside H₂¹⁶O. Because vibration frequencies depend on mass, the heavy species have slightly lower vapor pressures and condense preferentially, and this fractionation, compounded through successive evaporations and condensations as moisture moves poleward, makes the isotopic composition of polar snow a record of the temperature of its formation. The mechanism is developed in {doc}`water-molecule` and applied to ice cores in {doc}`../climate/paleoclimate`.

## Chemical bonds and their energies

Three kinds of binding are important in ice, and their relative magnitudes organize much of what follows. The covalent O–H bond within the molecule has an energy of about 460 kJ mol⁻¹. The hydrogen bond between molecules, in which a proton of one molecule is attracted to the lone-pair electrons of a neighbor, has an energy of roughly 20 kJ mol⁻¹ and is the bond that holds ice together. The van der Waals attractions present between all molecules, the dipolar, induction, and dispersion forces of the next chapter, contribute on the order of 1 kJ mol⁻¹. For comparison, the thermal energy $RT$ at the melting point is 2.3 kJ mol⁻¹. In the units more usual for single molecules, one electron volt per molecule corresponds to 96.5 kJ mol⁻¹, so the covalent bond is about 4.8 eV, the hydrogen bond about 0.2 eV, and $k_B T$ about 1/40 eV.

These magnitudes determine the phase behavior of water. Thermal energy at terrestrial temperatures is negligible compared with the covalent bond energy, so water molecules remain intact in all three phases. It is about a tenth of the hydrogen-bond energy, so hydrogen bonds are stable enough to maintain a crystal yet are broken at a small, finite rate, which is what permits creep, electrical conduction, and exchange with the vapor. It exceeds the van der Waals energy, so substances bound by dispersion forces alone, such as the H₂S of the preface's comparison, are gases at temperatures where water is solid. Ionic bonds, formed by the transfer of electrons as in NaCl, complete the classification; the polar water molecule dissolves ionic solids readily, which is the origin of sea salt and of the depressed freezing point of seawater treated in {doc}`../cryosphere/sea-ice`.

## The composition of natural ice

Natural ice is not pure H₂O. Polar ice is among the purest solid materials found in nature, with total impurity contents of parts per billion to parts per million by weight, but the impurities are glaciologically important out of proportion to their abundance. Sea salt, sulfate from volcanic eruptions, mineral dust, and acids from atmospheric chemistry are largely excluded from the crystal lattice, which offers no favorable site for foreign ions, and accumulate instead at grain boundaries and in the premelted liquid described in {doc}`ice-structure`. There they control the electrical conduction sensed by radar and supply the volcanic reference horizons used to date ice cores in {doc}`../climate/paleoclimate`. A few species, notably hydrogen fluoride and ammonia, can substitute into the lattice itself, where they act as dopants for the electrical defects of {doc}`point-defects`.

Glacier ice also contains air. The compaction of firn described in {doc}`snow-to-ice` seals roughly ten percent of the ice volume into bubbles of trapped atmosphere, which at sufficient depth and pressure convert to air-clathrate inclusions. The bubbles scatter light, which is why glacier ice is white while pure ice is transparent, and the air they hold is the only direct sample of past atmospheres. Natural ice is thus a crystal of H₂O containing measurable proportions of its own heavy isotopic varieties, a grain-boundary inventory of atmospheric impurities, and sealed samples of ancient air, and later chapters draw on each of these.
