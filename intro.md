# Introduction to Glaciology and Ice-Flow Modeling

Welcome to the Rice Glaciology group's open textbook. Here, we introduce the physics of glaciers and ice sheets, demonstrate how the physics of ice is encoded in numerical ice flow models like [icepack](https://icepack.github.io/), and walk through several examples of observations of the cryosphere that are actively shaping our understanding of Earth's ice sheets.

The book is aimed at advanced undergraduates and beginning graduate students who have taken some calculus and physics, but who are new to glaciology. We build up the ideas incrementally and try to introduce mathematics and physics using notebooks intended to gamify learning. Much of the material that we present borrows from standard references like *The Physics of Glaciers* {cite}`cuffey2010` and *Physics of Ice* {cite}`petrenko1999`; we point to specific chapters in these texts and other references for readers who want to dive deeper.

## First observers

The scientific study of ice sheets and glaciers is relatively young, but observations of glaciers and ice sheets are not. In the oral histories of the Māori tribal groups Ngāti Rārua and Te Āti Awa, the navigator Hui Te Rangiora sailed south from the Cook Islands on the vessel Te Ivi o Atea around 650 CE, into waters his crew described as *Te tai-uka-a-pia*, the sea foaming like arrowroot, the scraped white powder of pia being the closest thing in a tropical Pacific world to the appearance of floe ice {cite}`wehi2021`. The accounts speak of a white land that was floating, of bare summits piercing the sky, of animals that dive to great depths. These are likely some of the first observations of Antarctic ice shelves, and the marine life of the Southern Ocean, made and preserved roughly twelve centuries before the continent entered European charts. Ngāi Tahu and other iwi hold these voyaging histories among their treasured knowledge; a carved pou by the Ngāi Tahu artist Fayne Robinson, Te Kaiwhakatere o te Taki, the Navigator of the Heavens, has stood at Scott Base since 2013 with its face turned to the sky that guided those voyages; and Māori scholars today argue for an Antarctic future managed with Indigenous frameworks alongside the science {cite}`wehi2021policy`.

In the arctic, Sámi reindeer herders in northern Fennoscandia have maintained a taxonomy of snow featuring dozens of named snow types that distinguih the density, hardness, and layering of the snow. In Norway, now does not fall, it builds up, a reflection of the climate and the. Communities in the Andes, the Himalaya, Alaska, Greenland and Skandinavia have watched, named, and depended on their glaciers for centuries.

## For whom, and by whom?

It is fair to ask of any science who it serves and who gets to do it {cite}`robel2024`. Glaciological inquiry in the academy grew out of early expeditions from nineteenth-century Europe that continue to shape research agendas and access to the continent. Polar field sciences built a heroic self-image led almost exclusively by white men and mythologized as the conquest of hostile places. That image hardened into a gatekeeping conflation of scientific ability with outdoorsmanship that has no basis in the physics; as late as 2009, fewer than a fifth of the authors in the field's flagship journals were women, and a feminist analysis of the field's history and framing landed only in the last decade {cite}`carey2016`. 

The question of "for whom" has an even sharper present tense. The people most exposed to the cryosphere's changes are disproportionately Indigenous and are rarely at the table where the science is framed and shared. Demographic surveys of major polar collaborations remain overwhelmingly white {cite}`robel2024`. We keep this question open through the book. It has informed and motivated the writing. The tools here are deliberately open, and the content editable as part of a philosophy that embraces progressive evaluation of the science, science-history and understanding.

## How the book is organized

The chapters of our book build on one another and are ordered around the ingredients needed to solve equations that describe ice flow. We first develop a fundamental understanding of the water molecule and polycrystalline ice. This discussion builds to the formulation of momentum balance equations in ice flow models and the diagnostic problem of calculating the velocity of a glacier from the glacier's geometry. We then explore climate and model forcing, and identify the prognostic problem of evolving glacier geometry forward in time. Labs run throughout the whole book connecting these topics and applying mechanics to real-world glaciers.

**Getting started** introduces icepack, the flow-modeling package built on [Firedrake](https://www.firedrakeproject.org/) {cite}`shapero2021`, and the Docker image that runs it and every lab. **The physics of ice** builds ice from its atoms up, through the molecule, the crystal, its vibrations, its defects, and its optical properties, the scales where ice's rheology is set. **Ice deformation and flow** develops stress and strain, the nonlinear rheology, the momentum balance and its approximations, and solves the diagnostic problem with icepack on an ice shelf. **The bed and basal processes** adds heat flow, sliding, and subglacial hydrology, ending with a comparison of friction laws on a Thwaites-like glacier. **Mass balance and climate** covers accumulation and ablation, firn, glacier response theory, the ice sheets and their instabilities, and the climate archive in ice cores. **Prognostic modeling and climate forcing** makes the evolution problem concrete, the thickness equation marched forward in time under a parameterized climate. **Observing ice** works from raw data up, photon clouds, waveforms, interferograms, and seismograms processed into the velocity, elevation, and bed-geometry products the models consume, alongside the observations we would like to use more. **The wider cryosphere** closes with sea ice, former glaciers, permafrost, planetary ice, and finally sea level, where everything in the book runs downstream to the people waiting on the answer.

## Running the code

The modeling chapters use icepack, which runs on Firedrake. Firedrake can be awkward to install directly, so this book ships a **Docker image** that gives you a ready-to-go environment with Firedrake, icepack, and JupyterLab. See {doc}`sections/modeling/running-icepack-docker`. Because that stack is heavy, the notebooks are **not executed** when the book is built — you run them yourself in the container.
