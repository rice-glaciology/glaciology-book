# Preface

Water is the most familiar substance on Earth and one of the most anomalous. The chapters that follow breakdown the water molecule, and assemble the crystal. At each stage some of the unusual behavior we identify below will be traced to its molecular origin.

## The anomalies of water

Nearly every physical property of water is an outlier among small molecules:

Solid ice is less dense than water at temperatures (and ice phases) we experience on earth. Ice at 917 kg m⁻³ is about nine percent less dense than the water it freezes from, a reversal of the usual order of phases shared by only a handful of substances. The relative density of solid ice and liquid water allows lakes and oceans to freeze from the top down and support life in the liquid beneath, why ice shelves and sea ice exist at all, and why pressure favors melting at the bed of a thick ice sheet. The liquid compounds the strangeness by reaching its maximum density near 4 °C, so that cold lakes stratify with their warmest water at the bottom.

The melting and boiling points are far too high for the molecule's weight. Water's heavier chemical relatives set a clean trend, with H₂Te melting near −49 °C, H₂Se near −66 °C, and H₂S near −86 °C, and extrapolating that trend to water's mass predicts melting near −100 °C. The real molecule misses the prediction by a hundred degrees, and among molecules of comparable mass, methane melting at −182 °C, ammonia at −78 °C, water is the only one that is solid anywhere on Earth's surface. A planet at our distance from the Sun has abundant ice only because this anomaly exists.

Liquid water's heat capacity, about 75 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ or nine times the gas constant, is the largest of any common liquid; melting ice absorbs 334 kJ per kilogram without warming at all, and sublimation costs nearly an order of magnitude more. These numbers moderate Earth's climate, dominate the energy budget of every melting glacier surface, and make latent heat the largest entry in most of the balances this book will write down.

The remaining entries on the list will each find their chapter. Water expands on freezing with enough force to split bedrock, which is half of glacial erosion. The liquid can be supercooled tens of degrees before it freezes spontaneously, which is why mixed clouds of liquid droplets and ice crystals coexist and why snow forms the way it does. And both liquid and solid have enormous dielectric constants, near 80 for the liquid and roughly 100 for ice at low frequency, which makes water the universal solvent, gives ice the electrical liveliness treated in the defect chapters, and underlies every radar method in the observing part.

Each anomaly traces back, by arguments this part develops, to the same structural fact, a small bent molecule carrying two donatable protons and two acceptor lone pairs, which binds it to exactly four neighbors in an open tetrahedral network held by bonds strong enough to organize everything and weak enough to keep breaking. The list above is the evidence; the chapters that follow are the explanation.

## Thermal energy and equipartition

Reading the first chapter requires three statistical ideas, stated here in working form. Their full development, from the counting of microstates through the definition of temperature to the Boltzmann distribution, comes in {doc}`lattice-dynamics`, and nothing below depends on that machinery.

The first idea is that temperature sets a universal energy scale. A system at absolute temperature $T$ trades energy with its surroundings in packets of order $k_B T$, where $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant, and at the melting point of ice this thermal quantum is about $3.8\times10^{-21}$ joules, equivalently about 2.3 kJ per mole or one fortieth of an electron volt. Those three forms of the same number are worth memorizing, because every rate and every equilibrium in this book is ultimately a comparison between some energy cost and this thermal budget. A process costing much less than $k_B T$ happens freely; one costing many times $k_B T$ happens rarely, with a probability suppressed by the Boltzmann factor $e^{-E/k_B T}$ that later chapters use constantly.

The second idea is equipartition. In the classical limit, every independent quadratic contribution to a molecule's energy, each *degree of freedom*, holds an average energy of $\tfrac{1}{2}k_B T$. A molecule free to move in three dimensions carries three translational degrees of freedom; a rigid nonlinear molecule can additionally rotate about three distinct axes, adding three more. Equipartition then makes heat capacities countable. Each degree of freedom contributes $\tfrac{1}{2}R$ to the molar heat capacity at constant volume, and an ideal gas adds a further $R$ when heated at constant pressure, so a rigid bent molecule like water should show $C_p = \left(\tfrac{3}{2} + \tfrac{3}{2} + 1\right) R = 4R \approx 7.95$ cal mol⁻¹ K⁻¹. A *linear* molecule has one less usable rotation, since spinning about its own axis involves negligible moment of inertia, and should show only $\tfrac{7}{2}R \approx 6.95$. The measured value for water vapor, 8.65 cal mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ at 100 °C, sits squarely on the nonlinear prediction and rules out the linear one, which is how the molecule's bent shape was known from calorimetry alone, decades before spectroscopy could photograph it. The small excess above $4R$ is also informative, part vibration beginning to stir and part non-ideality of the vapor, a reminder that the clean fractions of equipartition are a limit rather than a law.

The third idea is that degrees of freedom can be frozen out, and this is where quantum mechanics first announces itself in this book, ahead of its proper introduction in {doc}`energy-levels`. A vibration of frequency $\nu$ cannot accept energy in amounts smaller than $h\nu$, so if $h\nu$ greatly exceeds $k_B T$ the mode cannot be excited at all and contributes nothing to the heat capacity, no matter what equipartition says. Water's internal vibrations have characteristic temperatures $h\nu/k_B$ of roughly 2,300 to 5,400 K, far above anything terrestrial, which is why they are absent from the 4R count above and why the molecule behaves as rigid in the argument that revealed its shape. The same freezing-out, applied mode by mode to the vibrations of the crystal, will explain the heat capacity of ice itself in {doc}`lattice-dynamics`, and the irreducible half-quantum $\tfrac{1}{2}h\nu$ that each mode keeps even at absolute zero, the zero-point energy, returns in {doc}`water-molecule` as the molecular engine of the isotope thermometer.

With the strangeness catalogued and the counting rules in hand, the explanation can begin, starting from the atoms themselves.
