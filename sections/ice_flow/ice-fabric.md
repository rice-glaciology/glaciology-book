# Ice fabric and the anisotropic flow law

Glen's law, as written in {doc}`ice-rheology`, treats ice as isotropic: its softness is the same in every direction. That assumption is a convenience, not a fact. A single ice crystal is one of the most mechanically anisotropic materials in nature, and as a glacier deforms its crystals rotate into preferred orientations that carry this anisotropy up to the scale of the flow. The result is that real ice can be many times softer for shear in one direction than the isotropic law predicts, and stiffer for compression along another. This chapter develops the description of crystal orientation fabric and shows how it turns the isotropic flow law into an anisotropic one. It is the last ingredient in the construction of a flow law, and it draws on the spectral fabric framework of Rathmann and colleagues {cite}`rathmann2021` and on Chapter 3 of {cite}`cuffey2010`.

## The single crystal, again

Recall from {doc}`../foundations/ice-structure` that the ice crystal has one special direction, the c-axis, perpendicular to the basal planes. The crystal glides easily on those planes, by the defect-mediated dislocation motion of {doc}`ice-rheology`, and resists shear across them by a large factor, of order sixty. A single crystal loaded in shear parallel to its basal plane therefore deforms readily, while the same crystal loaded so that the shear would have to cross the basal plane barely deforms at all. The c-axis points in the hard direction. If we know how the c-axes of all the crystals in a parcel of ice are arranged, we know, in principle, how that parcel will respond to stress. That arrangement is the crystal orientation fabric, and describing it quantitatively is the first task.

## Describing a fabric

Because only the c-axis direction matters for the mechanics, the orientation of a grain is a unit vector $\mathbf{c}$ on the sphere, with $\mathbf{c}$ and $-\mathbf{c}$ equivalent. A fabric is the statistical distribution of these axes over all the grains in a parcel, written as an orientation distribution function $f(\mathbf{c})$, normalized so that its integral over the sphere is one. The full function carries more detail than the flow law needs, and in practice the fabric is summarized by its moments, the orientation tensors formed by averaging outer products of $\mathbf{c}$ over the distribution. The most important is the second-order structure tensor,

$$
\mathbf{a}^{(2)} = \langle \mathbf{c}\otimes\mathbf{c}\rangle = \int f(\mathbf{c})\,\mathbf{c}\otimes\mathbf{c}\;\mathrm{d}^2\mathbf{c},
$$

a symmetric tensor whose trace is one. Its three eigenvalues $\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\lambda_3$ sum to unity and measure how much of the distribution points along each of its principal axes, and its eigenvectors give the directions of those axes. The second-order tensor is exactly the object that appears in the bulk dielectric and elastic properties of ice, which is why the same $\langle\mathbf{c}^2\rangle$ governs both the flow and the radar response in {doc}`../radar/radiowave-fabric`. For the nonlinear flow law a fourth-order tensor $\mathbf{a}^{(4)}=\langle\mathbf{c}\otimes\mathbf{c}\otimes\mathbf{c}\otimes\mathbf{c}\rangle$ is also needed, because the stress enters the rheology to a power.

Three archetypes organize the possibilities. An isotropic fabric, with axes pointing equally in all directions, has $\mathbf{a}^{(2)}=\tfrac13\mathbf{I}$ and eigenvalues $(\tfrac13,\tfrac13,\tfrac13)$; the bulk ice behaves like the isotropic Glen material. A single-maximum fabric, with the axes clustered about one direction, has one eigenvalue approaching one and the others approaching zero; this is the strongly anisotropic case. A girdle fabric, with the axes spread in a plane, has two comparable eigenvalues and one small one. The progression from the isotropic state toward a single maximum or a girdle is the progression from Glen's law toward a strongly directional rheology, and the eigenvalues are a compact measure of how far along that progression a given parcel of ice has gone.

## How fabric develops

Fabric is not imposed; it grows as ice deforms, through a competition among three processes. The first is lattice rotation. As a parcel strains, each grain's c-axis rotates like a material line embedded in the flow, turning away from the axis of extension and toward the axis of compression. Vertical compression near an ice divide therefore drives the c-axes toward the vertical, building a single maximum, while horizontal extension spreads them into a girdle in the horizontal plane. This kinematic rotation, driven by the velocity gradient, is the dominant fabric-forming mechanism in cold ice and would, by itself, sharpen the fabric without limit.

The second process is recrystallization, which intervenes to reorganize the grains. At low temperatures and stresses, grains subdivide as dislocations arrange into new boundaries, a rotation recrystallization, or polygonization, that nucleates daughter grains with slightly rotated axes and tends to spread a fabric out. At high temperatures and stresses, near the melting point and in fast-deforming ice, grain boundaries sweep through the crystal and consume grains that are unfavorably oriented, a migration recrystallization that favors orientations soft for the imposed stress and can produce strong, sometimes multi-maximum, fabrics quickly. The third process is normal grain growth, the slow coarsening that reduces total boundary area and proceeds steadily with age and temperature. Which process dominates depends on temperature, stress, and strain, and the observed fabrics in ice cores, from the broadening girdles of the upper column to the tight single maxima of deep, warm ice, record their interplay {cite}`cuffey2010`.

The patterns line up with the stress regimes of a glacier. Beneath a divide, vertical compression makes a vertical single maximum, which is why deep divide ice is soft in horizontal shear. In the bed-parallel simple shear of an ice stream or the deep ice of a sheet, the c-axes cluster toward the vertical and slightly downstream, aligning the easy basal planes with the plane of shearing. In the lateral shear of a stream margin, a strong horizontal maximum develops across the flow. Each of these fabrics is both a product of the local deformation and, through the flow law below, a control on it.

## From grains to a bulk flow law

To turn a fabric into a flow law we must average the response of the individual grains, each of which obeys the strongly anisotropic single-crystal rheology, into the response of the polycrystal. This homogenization is the central problem, and it is bounded by two idealized assumptions. In the Sachs bound every grain feels the same stress, deforms by basal glide, and the polycrystal strain rate is the orientation average of the grain strain rates; this treats the grains as mechanically independent and gives the softer, lower bound on bulk strength. In the Taylor bound every grain undergoes the same strain, which requires each to activate hard non-basal slip to maintain compatibility with its neighbors, and gives the stiffer, upper bound. Real ice lies between, and practical fabric models interpolate between the bounds with a calibration to data {cite}`rathmann2021`.

The outcome of the averaging is expressed through directional enhancement factors, the ratios by which a given fabric softens or stiffens the strain rate for a given mode of stress relative to isotropic ice. For a vertical single maximum the enhancement to bed-parallel simple shear is large, of order eight to ten, because the aligned basal planes are all oriented to glide, while the enhancement to vertical uniaxial compression is less than one, because deforming along the c-axes requires the hard mode. Inserting these enhancements turns Glen's law into a transversely isotropic generalization, in which the scalar fluidity is replaced by an orientation-dependent tensor built from the structure tensors $\mathbf{a}^{(2)}$ and $\mathbf{a}^{(4)}$ and a small number of enhancement parameters. The isotropic Glen's law is recovered when $\mathbf{a}^{(2)}=\tfrac13\mathbf{I}$, so the anisotropic law contains the isotropic one as its random-fabric limit.

The mechanical consequence is sharpest in shear margins, where a feedback operates. Concentrated lateral shear builds a strong horizontal fabric, the fabric enhances the shear, the enhanced shear concentrates further, and the margin becomes a narrow band of soft, fast-shearing ice. Treating such a margin with the isotropic law underestimates the deformation by close to the enhancement factor, which is why the fabric cannot be neglected where the dynamics matter most. The same is true at divides, where the soft vertical-maximum ice affects the depth-age relation that dates the ice cores drilled there.

## Modeling fabric and what it connects to

Because fabric and flow are coupled, a model that resolves the feedback must carry the orientation distribution as a field and evolve it with the flow. The efficient way to do this is spectral: expand the distribution function $f(\mathbf{c})$ in spherical harmonics and step the expansion coefficients forward under lattice rotation, recrystallization, and grain growth, recovering the structure tensors and the bulk anisotropic viscosity from the low-order coefficients. The specfab framework of {cite}`rathmann2021` implements exactly this, and because the same expansion gives the bulk dielectric and elastic tensors, it ties the deformation model directly to what polarimetric radar and seismic surveys measure. This is the bridge from the flow law back to observation: the fabric that softens the ice also makes it electromagnetically and acoustically anisotropic, so the fabric inferred from a radar survey, by the travel-time methods of {doc}`../radar/radiowave-fabric`, can be fed into the flow law, and the flow law in turn predicts the fabric a survey should see.

With fabric in hand the construction of the flow law is complete. We began with the deformation of a single crystal, built the isotropic Glen's law from the statistics of many randomly oriented grains and the defect physics that limits their glide, added the temperature dependence through the rate factor, and have now restored the directional softening that develops as the grains align. What remains is to put this rheology into the momentum balance, which is the work of the chapters that follow.
