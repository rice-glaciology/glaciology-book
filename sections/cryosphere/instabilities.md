# Flow instabilities: surges and the tidewater cycle

The kinematic theory of {doc}`../climate/glacier-variations` rests on a quiet assumption, that a glacier's length changes only because its mass balance changes. Most glaciers honor it. This chapter is about the ones that do not, glaciers whose termini advance and retreat by kilometers with little or no prompting from climate, because the instability lives inside the glacier itself. There are two great families. Surge-type glaciers oscillate because of a switch at the bed. Tidewater glaciers cycle because of a conversation between ice, sediment, and seawater at the terminus. Both matter to anyone reading glacier records for climate, because both produce dramatic length changes that mean nothing about the weather, and both are treated in Chapter 12 of {cite}`cuffey2010`.

## Surge-type glaciers

A surge is defined by its rhythm. For most of a cycle lasting decades to a century, a surge-type glacier flows sluggishly, more slowly than balance would require, while ice piles up in an upper reservoir zone and the lower glacier stagnates and thins. Then, for a year or two, the glacier flows ten to a thousand times faster, transferring a large fraction of its volume down-glacier into the receiving zone, often advancing the terminus by kilometers, after which the flow shuts down and the buildup begins again. Surging glaciers are rare and geographically clustered, with hundreds in Alaska and the Yukon, dozens in Svalbard, the Pamirs, and Iceland, and almost none elsewhere {cite}`meier1969,sevestre2015`. The clustering by region, and by the climatic and geometric middle ground those regions share, is the strongest clue that surging is a property of the thermal and hydrological regime of the bed. The thermal switch that governs the Svalbard-style surges, and the relaxation oscillation it produces, were treated with the heat flow in {doc}`../thermomechanics/thermal-structure`. Here we take up the hydrological switch, through the glacier that showed everyone how it works.

### Variegated Glacier, 1982–83

Variegated Glacier, a twenty-kilometer valley glacier near Yakutat Bay in Alaska, had surged roughly every two decades since 1906, so in 1973 a team of glaciologists instrumented it and settled in to wait {cite}`kamb1985`. The wait itself was instructive. Over the quiescent decade the reservoir zone thickened and steepened, the velocity crept upward year by year, and curious precursors appeared, mini-surges in which a pulse of high basal water pressure traveled down-glacier at around 400 meters per hour with a brief burst of fast sliding riding on top of it. The cumulative mass balance climbed toward the same threshold, about 43 meters of ice, that had preceded earlier surges, a geometric trigger rather than a climatic one.

The surge arrived in two phases beginning in January 1982 and climaxed in June 1983 with the lower glacier moving at 65 meters per day. Three observations from that summer carry most of the physics. First, boreholes showed that the surface and the bed moved at nearly the same speed, so the glacier was sliding as a plug, and transverse profiles that had been parabolic in quiet years became flat-topped, with fast ice extending to within meters of the walls. Whatever had changed had changed at the bed. Second, dye poured into moulins during the surge took weeks to reach the terminus, smeared into a long tail, while dye injected days after the surge ended raced through in a day. The drainage system during the surge was slow, distributed, and pressurized, the linked cavities of {doc}`../thermomechanics/hydrology`, and the system after it was a fast, efficient conduit. Third, the surge terminated abruptly, everywhere, on the Fourth of July 1983, accompanied by an enormous flood from the terminus stream. The water that had been stored under the glacier, holding it off its bed, left all at once.

The mechanism reads directly out of the observations. In quiescence the glacier drains through a few large conduits that keep basal water pressure low and the ice coupled to its bed. As the reservoir steepens and the flow quickens, the sliding begins to disrupt conduits faster than melting can maintain them, and the drainage collapses into linked cavities whose pressure rises with throughput, in the inverse relation derived in {doc}`../thermomechanics/hydrology`. High pressure means low effective pressure, fast sliding, more cavity opening, and the feedback runs away. The surge ends when the swollen cavity system finally connects through to an efficient channel, the stored water escapes as the observed flood, the effective pressure recovers, and the bed grips again. The surge is a drainage-system oscillation, with the glacier's geometry as the slowly loaded spring.

## The tidewater glacier cycle

A tidewater glacier ends in the sea, grounded, with a calving face. Its instability needs no switch at the bed; it is built into the long profile of the fjord the glacier itself excavates.

```{figure} figures/tidewater-cycle.svg

:width: 88%

The tidewater glacier cycle. (a) A glacier advances slowly down its fjord behind a protective moraine shoal built by its own subglacial streams, at a pace set by sediment supply. (b) A small retreat off the shoal exposes the terminus to deep water. (c) Calving increases with water depth, and retreat through the overdeepened basin is self-sustaining until the terminus regains shallow water at the fjord head. After Chapter 12 of {cite}`cuffey2010`.
```

The advancing phase is paced not by ice dynamics but by earthmoving. Subglacial streams deliver sediment to the terminus, building a moraine shoal that shields the ice from deep water and from the warm seawater that would otherwise melt and undercut it. The glacier advances down the fjord at meters per year, the rate at which the shoal can be pushed forward. At Taku Glacier near Juneau, the one major Alaskan tidewater glacier recently in its advancing phase, soundings show the terminus excavating one to four meters of sediment per year as it plows forward over an inlet that was navigable fjord a century ago {cite}`motyka2006`.

The retreat is the fast half of the cycle. A modest perturbation, perhaps a few warm summers, backs the terminus off its shoal into the deeper basin behind, water the glacier itself overdeepened during earlier advances. Calving rate increases sharply with water depth, so retreat begets deeper water and deeper water begets faster retreat, a positive feedback with the same logical shape as the marine ice-sheet instability of {doc}`ice-sheets`, scaled to a fjord. There is no stable terminus position in the overdeepening, and the retreat runs until the glacier finds shallow water again at the head of the fjord {cite}`meier1987,pfeffer2007`.

The calving that drives the fast half of the cycle is worth watching once at full violence. The clip below shows a calving event at Helheim Glacier in southeast Greenland, kilometre-scale blocks rotating out of a face hundreds of metres tall.

Columbia Glacier is the canonical witness. Mapped on its shoal at Heather Island by the Harriman Expedition in 1899, it was still there in 1969, then slipped off around 1980 and has since retreated more than twenty kilometers up its fjord, at times among the fastest-flowing glaciers on Earth. That single glacier accounted for nearly half of the ice loss from the entire western Chugach range over the period {cite}`berthier2010`, while immediate neighbors, lacking the tidewater configuration, barely changed. Glacier Bay tells the same story at larger scale, a hundred kilometers of retreat since 1794, and its sediment-choked inlets show the recovery phase already in preparation, shoals on which some future advance will ride. The cycle turns on timescales of centuries, only loosely synchronized with climate.

```{admonition} Reading length records, with care
:class: warning

A tidewater glacier in its retreat phase tells you about its fjord, not about the climate of the past decade, and a surge-type glacier's advance tells you about its plumbing. Attribution arguments of the kind made in {doc}`../climate/glacier-variations` are built on populations of ordinary land-terminating glaciers precisely to dodge these two families. Before reading any single spectacular retreat as climate, ask first what the terminus is standing in, and what the bed is made of.
```

## What the instabilities have in common

Each of these systems pairs a slowly accumulating reservoir with a fast, thresholded release, ice piled above a drainage switch in the surge cycle, a terminus perched on a shoal above an overdeepening in the tidewater cycle, and an interior leaning on a sill in the marine ice-sheet case. All three are relaxation oscillators or runaway feedbacks built from the same parts, mass conservation plus a flux law with the wrong sign of slope somewhere. The mathematics of this chapter and the last is deliberately small, but it is the right mathematics, and when the icepack labs evolve marine glaciers across overdeepened beds you will see these diagrams come to life.
