# Planetary ice

Everything derived in this book was motivated by ice on Earth, but the physics is not confined to it. The continuity equation, Glen's flow law, the heat equation, the Stefan condition are indifferent to location. Water is the most abundant volatile in the solar system after hydrogen and helium, and at the temperatures that prevail beyond the frost line, roughly 2 to 3 AU from the Sun, liquid water is a local and temporary exception. Ice is the default state. The solar system holds more water ice by mass than all the world's oceans combined, distributed across polar caps, permafrost mantles, buried glaciers, ocean-covered moons, and the nuclei of comets. The diagnostic and prognostic problems this book has been building — what is the thermal structure, how does the material deform, what does the stratigraphic record say about past climate — are the same questions planetary scientists are asking at dozens of icy worlds. This chapter walks the machinery off the Earth and sees how it travels.

## Mars

Mars is the most Earth-like of the other planets in its ice inventory, and it repays a careful look because each of its icy features has a direct analog in a chapter we have already read.

The Martian poles are capped year-round by layered deposits of water ice and dust that reach 3 km thick at the north pole and slightly less at the south. Unlike the seasonal carbon dioxide frost that comes and goes with every Martian year, the polar layered deposits are permanent, and they are a paleoclimate archive in the same sense as the Antarctic or Greenland ice sheets treated in {doc}`../climate/paleoclimate`. Obliquity cycles on Mars span roughly 120,000 years, the eccentricity cycle near 95,000 years, and the precession cycle near 51,000 years — a Milankovitch spectrum shifted to different periods but the same orbital logic. Dust and water ice accumulate in proportions that vary with insolation, and the resulting stratigraphy is visible in the steep scarps that erosion has cut into the deposit, alternating bright and dark layers that are the Martian equivalent of the glacial–interglacial cycles readable in an ice core.

The tool for reading that stratigraphy from orbit is radar sounding, exactly the electromagnetic framework of {doc}`../radar/em-waves`. MARSIS (the Mars Advanced Radar for Subsurface and Ionosphere Sounding, on Mars Express) and SHARAD (the Shallow Radar, on Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter) send pulses at frequencies of 1–5 MHz and 20 MHz respectively and record the travel time and amplitude of reflections from internal layers and from the base of the deposit. The dielectric contrast between ice-rich and dust-rich layers is modest, so faint internal reflectors are right at the sensitivity limit; the basal reflector, where water ice meets the silicate substrate, is strong. SHARAD has mapped the three-dimensional layer architecture across the entire north polar cap, resolving beds tens of meters thick and tracing them laterally for hundreds of kilometers — a radar stratigraphy campaign of a scope that has no terrestrial equal, though the physics of wave propagation in the deposit is the same as in an Antarctic sounding survey.

Away from the poles, Mars is a permafrost planet. The mean surface temperature is near −50 °C, and the geothermal gradient carries the subsurface back to the melting point of water only at depths of tens of kilometers in most regions, so the frozen ground is kilometers thick rather than the hundreds of meters seen in the coldest parts of Siberia ({doc}`../thermomechanics/frozen-ground`). Phoenix landed in the northern plains in 2008 and scraped back a few centimeters of soil to expose clean water ice within the active layer, the same architecture — a seasonally thawed skin over a deep frozen column — that the trumpet diagram of frozen ground describes. High-resolution imaging from orbit shows polygonal patterned ground at high Martian latitudes indistinguishable in plan-view geometry from the thermal-contraction polygons of the Arctic tundra; the same ratchet of winter cracking and spring-melt infilling that builds ice wedges on Earth appears to operate there. Radar sounding at mid-latitudes has also detected lobate debris aprons — glacier-like bodies buried under a thin lag of rocky debris — whose radar stratigraphy shows internal layering consistent with clean ice. These are the Martian analogs of the debris-covered glaciers that appear in alpine and periglacial settings, ice that survives because the rocky cover insulates it from sublimation just as debris insulates glaciers on Earth.

Mars also has a seasonal CO₂ ice cap that condenses at the poles in autumn as temperatures drop below the CO₂ frost point near 148 K and sublimes in spring, storing and releasing enough gas to change the atmospheric pressure by 25% over a Martian year. Carbon dioxide is a different substance from water, but it obeys the same thermodynamics. The CO₂ frost point on the phase diagram — the pressure-temperature curve below which the solid is stable against sublimation — is the analog of the water phase diagram whose features are rooted in the lattice dynamics of {doc}`../foundations/lattice-dynamics`. The seasonal cap is not a glaciological object in the flow sense, since CO₂ ice forms as a surface deposit and sublimates before it can accumulate to flow-relevant thicknesses, but it is a reminder that the phase diagram is the organizing principle of planetary ice regardless of molecular species.

## Icy moons

The outer solar system holds several moons with global liquid-water oceans beneath thick ice shells, and they are arguably the most interesting icy objects in the solar system for the combination of physics they involve.

Europa, the smallest of Jupiter's Galilean moons, has a surface of nearly pure water ice cut by a network of ridges, bands, and chaotic terrain that records deformation of the shell from below. The shell is roughly 10 to 30 km thick, with a liquid ocean of perhaps 100 km depth beneath it. Enceladus, a small moon of Saturn, is even more dramatic: geysers of water vapor and ice crystals erupt from fractures near its south pole, supplying material to Saturn's E ring and providing direct chemical samples of a subsurface ocean. The energy source for both moons is tidal dissipation. Europa is locked in an orbital resonance with Io and Ganymede that forces its orbit to remain slightly eccentric, so Jupiter's gravity kneads it periodically; Enceladus is in resonance with Dione. The tidal strain rate is small, but because the material is viscous, the dissipation is non-trivial and is the reason these moons have not frozen solid.

The ice shell of Europa sits between two heat sources: tidal dissipation within the ice and, potentially, hydrothermal flux from the seafloor below. Whether the shell convects or simply conducts heat controls its thickness and the vigor of any vertical transport. The competition is the same one the Rayleigh and Peclet numbers express for terrestrial ice sheets ({doc}`../thermomechanics/thermal-structure`). A purely conductive shell loses heat at a rate set by its thickness and the thermal conductivity of cold ice; a convecting shell turns over, transporting heat more efficiently, and tends to be thicker for a given heat flux. The relevant dimensionless group is a Rayleigh number built from the shell thickness, the thermal expansion coefficient, and the temperature-dependent viscosity — the viscosity being the core issue, since cold ice near 100 K has a homologous temperature $T/T_m$ well below 0.9, the range where Glen's law ({doc}`../ice_flow/ice-rheology`) was constrained by laboratory and field measurements on Earth. At low homologous temperatures the creep rate drops precipitously, making the upper part of the shell essentially rigid and concentrating any convective motion in a softer layer near the base. Whether that basal layer is warm enough and thick enough to convect is not settled observationally, and it matters because a convecting shell mixes chemical tracers between the surface and the ocean, whereas a purely conductive shell does not.

The ocean access problem is central to astrobiology: if Europa or Enceladus hosts life in its ocean, can biosignatures reach the surface where a spacecraft could detect them? For Europa the answer depends on whether convection, impact gardening, or some other process can bring ocean material to the surface on timescales shorter than the radiation damage timescale of the ice. For Enceladus the plumes provide a direct sample — the Cassini spacecraft flew through them repeatedly and detected organic molecules, silica nanoparticles indicative of hydrothermal activity, and molecular hydrogen, a chemical disequilibrium that could sustain chemolithotrophs. The physics of the plumes involves fracture mechanics of the ice shell and the pressure of the ocean driving fluid up the fractures, a subglacial-hydrology problem in a radically different setting but with the same force balance between water pressure and overburden ({doc}`../thermomechanics/hydrology`).

## Nitrogen glaciers of Pluto

When New Horizons flew past Pluto in July 2015, it revealed a heart-shaped basin, Tombaugh Regio, whose western lobe — Sputnik Planitia — is filled with nitrogen ice to a depth of several kilometers. The surface is divided into polygonal cells roughly 20 to 40 km across, each rising slightly at its edges and slightly lower at its center, a pattern unmistakable to anyone who has studied convection. The cells are the surface expression of Rayleigh–Bénard convection in a thick layer of nitrogen ice heated from below by Pluto's residual internal heat, cooled from above by radiation to the sky at 40 K. The turnover time is estimated at tens of thousands of years, which is geologically fast even by the standards of warm terrestrial ice.

Nitrogen ice at Pluto's surface conditions has a creep rheology that has been measured in the laboratory. The flow law has the same power-law form as Glen's law,

$$
\dot\varepsilon = A(\tau)\,\tau^n,
$$

where $\dot\varepsilon$ is the strain rate, $\tau$ the deviatoric stress, $n$ the stress exponent, and $A(\tau)$ the rate factor; the equation sets the speed of creep by the stress raised to the power $n$, scaled by $A$. The material parameters differ from water ice, with $n \approx 2$ for N₂ ice near its triple point and a rate factor $A$ orders of magnitude larger than for water ice at the same homologous temperature, which is why Sputnik Planitia can convect and deform visibly on million-year timescales despite Pluto's feeble heat flux. Nitrogen glaciers also flow off the edges of the basin down into the surrounding terrain, and their profiles obey the shallow-ice approximation ({doc}`../ice_flow/shallow-ice`) just as terrestrial valley glaciers do — the same depth-integrated force balance, the same relationship between surface slope and flux, just with a different viscosity and a gravity of 0.063g. The continuum machinery is indifferent to the molecular identity of the material.

## Amorphous ice and high-pressure phases

Everything so far in this book has been hexagonal ice Ih, the stable phase at the pressures and temperatures found on Earth's surface. The phase diagram sketched in {doc}`../foundations/lattice-dynamics` shows that water ice has at least seventeen crystalline polymorphs, most of them stable only at pressures exceeding a few hundred megapascals. In the icy moons those pressures are reached within the first hundred kilometers of depth. Ganymede, Callisto, and Titan are large enough that the base of their water-ice layers reaches the stability field of ice III, ice V, and ice VI, and a fully differentiated Ganymede may have ice VII at the base of its approximately 800 km deep water layer. The rheology and thermal conductivity of high-pressure ice phases differ substantially from ice Ih, which changes the heat transport calculation and the thickness of the overlying ocean.

At the other extreme, comets carry amorphous ice — water molecules quenched from the gas phase in the interstellar medium or the early solar nebula so rapidly that they never organized into a crystal lattice. Amorphous ice is metastable and crystallizes irreversibly to cubic or hexagonal ice above roughly 130 K, releasing a small burst of latent heat as it does. When a comet crosses the frost line on its inbound orbit, the shallow subsurface warms past 130 K, amorphous-to-crystalline conversion produces heat, and trapped volatiles (CO, CO₂, more volatile species) that were locked in the amorphous matrix are expelled. This transition is one of the mechanisms thought to drive cometary outbursts — sudden brightening events that occur well beyond the water-sublimation zone, where solar heating alone cannot explain the activity.

The polymorphism connects back to the fundamental picture developed in {doc}`../foundations/lattice-dynamics` and {doc}`../foundations/ice-structure`: the hydrogen bond network is flexible enough to accommodate many different packing arrangements depending on the pressure and temperature of formation, each with its own density, conductivity, and stiffness. Ice is not one material; it is a family.

## Summary

This book opened with the peculiar geometry of the water molecule and the hydrogen bond it forms, and every chapter since has been an elaboration on what follows from that. The phase diagram, the crystal lattice, the dislocation creep that Glen's law parametrizes, the thermal structure that sets basal conditions, the radar wave that reads internal stratigraphy, the isotopic signal that speaks of ancient temperature — all of it threads back to H₂O at whatever pressure and temperature a particular world provides. The diagnostic problem (what is the current state of the ice) and the prognostic problem (where is it going) are the same whether the ice is the West Antarctic Ice Sheet, the north polar cap of Mars, or the shell of Europa. The tools are transferable; only the boundary conditions change.

Ice is also the solar system's most legible archive. It accumulates in layers, it preserves chemical tracers without chemical reaction, it moves slowly enough that the record persists for millions of years, and it is transparent to the radar frequencies needed to read it without drilling. Every icy world is a stratigraphy waiting to be interpreted, and the interpretive framework is the one you have just spent a course building. The physics derived here for a valley glacier in Alaska or an ice core from Dome C is the same physics that will eventually be applied, by the missions already in development, to the layered deposits of Mars and the shells of the outer planets. The cryosphere is not a terrestrial specialty. It is solar-system science, and you now have its foundations.
