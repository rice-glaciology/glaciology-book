# Optical and dielectric properties of ice

The molecule and the lattice that gave ice its structure, its vibrations, and its defects also determine how it responds to an electromagnetic field, and that response is the basis of nearly every method used to observe the cryosphere. Radar sounds the depth of an ice sheet, satellite altimeters range to its surface, and polarimetric radar reads its crystal fabric, all by sending electromagnetic waves into the ice and timing what returns. This chapter develops the property those methods depend on, the permittivity of ice and the refractive index that follows from it, from the molecular physics of the earlier chapters. It collects the optical and dielectric behaviour of the crystal in one place so that the observing chapters, beginning with {doc}`../radar/em-waves`, can treat wave propagation without stopping to derive the material response. It draws on the dipole and polarizability of {doc}`water-molecule`, the vibrational spectrum of {doc}`lattice-dynamics`, and the orientational defects of {doc}`point-defects`, and follows {cite}`fletcher1970`, {cite}`petrenko1999`, and {cite}`bogorodsky1985`.

## Polarization and permittivity

When an electric field is applied to ice the charges within it shift, the electron clouds distort, the atoms displace, and the molecular dipoles turn, and the sum of these displacements is the polarization. The permittivity $\epsilon$ measures how much polarization a given field produces, and it is the single material property that governs the propagation of an electromagnetic wave, which travels at speed $v=c/n$ with refractive index $n=\sqrt{\epsilon'}$, the prime denoting the real part. The decisive point is that the three contributions to the polarization respond on very different timescales. The electrons are light and respond almost instantaneously, up to optical frequencies. The atoms are heavier and respond up to infrared frequencies, the frequencies of the lattice vibrations. The molecular dipoles are slowest of all, because turning a molecule in the ice lattice is a sluggish, defect-mediated process. As the frequency of an applied wave rises, these mechanisms drop out one by one, each unable to keep up, and the permittivity falls in steps. Reading the permittivity from low frequency to high is therefore reading off which response is still active.

## Electronic polarization and the optical window

At the highest frequencies, in the visible, only the electrons can follow the field. The distortion of the electron clouds of the water molecules gives ice a permittivity of about $1.72$ and a refractive index of about $1.31$, close to that of liquid water, and this is why ice is transparent to visible light and why a glacier looks like what it is, frozen water. The index varies only slightly across the visible, from about $1.306$ in the red to $1.318$ in the violet, a weak dispersion. Ice in this window is almost lossless, but not quite: the faint residual absorption is stronger for red light than for blue, the high-frequency tail of the vibrational absorption discussed next reaching up from the infrared. Light that travels far through clean ice therefore loses its red end and emerges blue, which is the origin of the deep blue of thick glacier ice and crevasse walls.

## Vibrational polarization and the infrared

Below the visible, the atoms begin to contribute. The lattice and molecular vibrations catalogued in {doc}`lattice-dynamics` are the motions of charged atoms, so an oscillating field at their frequencies drives them resonantly and is absorbed. The intramolecular stretches and bend of the covalent bonds absorb in the near infrared, the librations of whole molecules in the mid infrared, and the translational lattice modes in the far infrared. Across this whole band, between the visible and the radio, ice is opaque, and the permittivity steps up from its optical value as each set of modes adds its share of polarization. The infrared spectrum of ice is, in this sense, the same vibrational spectrum that sets its heat capacity, seen through its coupling to light rather than to heat.

## Orientational polarization and dielectric relaxation

The largest response of all comes from the permanent dipole of the water molecule. In a slowly varying field the molecules reorient to point their dipoles along it, and because water's dipole is large and the molecules are densely packed, the resulting polarization is enormous: the static permittivity of ice is about $100$, far larger than the optical value. But this alignment is slow. A molecule cannot turn without breaking its bonding rules with its neighbours, so it can reorient only as a Bjerrum defect passes through, at the pace of defect migration set out in {doc}`point-defects`. The characteristic time for this is of order $10^{-4}$ seconds, so once the field oscillates faster than a few kilohertz the dipoles can no longer follow it. The permittivity then drops, through the Debye relaxation, from its static value near $100$ to a high-frequency value of about $3.2$, and it is in this relaxation, in the kilohertz range, that ice absorbs electromagnetic energy most strongly. The relaxation time lengthens as the ice cools and shortens with the impurity content, so the dielectric loss of ice carries a record of its temperature and chemistry.

## The permittivity spectrum and the two windows

Assembling these contributions gives the permittivity of ice as a staircase descending with frequency. It begins near $100$ at low frequency, where dipoles, atoms, and electrons all respond; falls through the Debye relaxation in the kilohertz range as the dipoles drop out, to a plateau of about $3.2$ that it holds through the radio and microwave range; steps down again across the infrared as the vibrations drop out; and settles at about $1.72$ in the visible, where only the electrons remain, before reaching unity in the ultraviolet. Two flat, low-loss stretches stand out. The radio and microwave plateau at $3.2$, with refractive index $1.78$, is the radar window through which radio waves travel kilometres into cold ice, the basis of radio-echo sounding and phase-sensitive radar. The visible plateau at $1.72$ is the optical window through which we see ice and through which laser altimeters range to its surface. The absorbing infrared band between them is the price of the vibrations. Every electromagnetic observing method works in one window or the other, and its physics is set by the value of the permittivity there.

## Optical and dielectric anisotropy

Ice Ih has a single symmetry axis, the c-axis of {doc}`ice-structure`, and its electromagnetic response is slightly different along that axis than across it. The crystal is uniaxial: its refractive index and its permittivity each take one value for fields parallel to the c-axis and another for fields perpendicular to it. The difference is small, at radio frequencies an anisotropy $\Delta\epsilon$ of only a few percent of the mean permittivity of about $3.2$, but it is enough to make a wave travel at a speed that depends on its polarization relative to the crystal axis. In a single crystal this is ordinary optical birefringence. In a glacier, where the bulk response is the average over many grains, it means that the bulk permittivity is anisotropic whenever the crystal fabric is aligned, and a radio wave splits into fast and slow polarizations that drift apart with depth. Measuring that splitting is how polarimetric radar recovers the crystal fabric, the method developed in {doc}`../radar/radiowave-fabric`. With the permittivity, its spectrum, and its anisotropy established here from the molecular physics, the propagation of electromagnetic waves through ice, and the instruments that exploit it, follow in the observing chapters.
