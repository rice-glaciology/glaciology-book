# Observing the cryosphere

Everything in the earlier parts of this book is theory, and theory is only as good as the observations that test it. A flow model needs a geometry to run on and a velocity to be judged against; an inversion for the bed needs a surface velocity to fit; a forecast of sea-level rise needs to know whether the ice sheets are in balance now. All of this comes from observation, and the observing of ice has grown in a generation from a few stakes in the snow into a dense web of satellites and ground instruments. This part surveys the main techniques, what each one measures, how the raw data are turned into a glaciological quantity, and what they have revealed. Each method has its own chapter; this one lays out the landscape.

It helps to organize the methods by what they measure. Four quantities matter most. The first is the geometry of the ice surface and how it changes, which gives the thickness and slope that drive the flow and the elevation change that signals imbalance. It is measured from orbit by laser altimetry in {doc}`laser-altimetry`, by radar altimetry in {doc}`radar-altimetry`, and by stereo photogrammetry from high-resolution panchromatic imagery in {doc}`panchromatic-imagery`. The second is the motion of the surface, the velocity field that a diagnostic model must reproduce and from which the bed is inferred. It is mapped by radar interferometry in {doc}`insar` and by tracking features between optical images. The third is the internal structure of the ice and the properties of its bed, sounded by the ice-penetrating radar of {doc}`../radar/em-waves`, {doc}`../radar/apres`, and {doc}`radiowave-fabric`. The fourth is what lies at and beneath the bed, the water, sediment, and rock that set the basal boundary condition, probed by the passive seismology of {doc}`cryoseismicity`, the magnetotellurics of {doc}`magnetotellurics`, and the gravity measurements of {doc}`gravity`.

Two broad families run through these chapters. Remote sensing from satellites and aircraft gives wide, repeated coverage of the surface and the shallow subsurface, and it is what turned glaciology into a science of whole ice sheets observed through time. Ground-based geophysics gives depth: seismic, electromagnetic, and gravity methods reach through the ice to the bed and below, at the cost of covering only the few places an expedition can reach. The two are complementary, the satellites mapping the surface expression of a process and the ground geophysics revealing the machinery beneath, and the strongest results in the field usually come from combining them. Each chapter that follows pairs the physical principle of a method with a worked data-processing example, so that the observation can be read not as a finished map but as a measurement with its own assumptions and limits.
